## Product
Olive Tree Bible App — a cross-platform Bible-study application (Mac, PC, iPad, iPhone, Android). The free app is the base; revenue comes from an in-app store of paid digital resources plus subscriptions.

Pricing model is hybrid:
- **One-time purchases** (à la carte digital books, sold through the in-app store):
  - Bible translations: "The Message (MSG)" $9.99, "NASB1995" $9.99, "KJV with Strong's Numbers" $19.99, "Amplified Bible Classic" $19.99
  - Study Bibles: "ESV Study Bible" $39.99, "NLT Study Bible" $11.99 (sale, from $29.99)
  - Reference / devotionals: "Naves Topical Bible Index" $2.99, "Spurgeon's Morning and Evening" $4.99, "Thayer's Greek-English Lexicon" $9.99
  - Premium collections: "Pillar Commentary" $249.99 (from $994.81), "Christ-Centered Exposition Commentary" $129.99 (from $560.57), "Old and New Testament Library Series" $399.99 (from $2,775.29), "BDAG and HALOT Collection" $199.99 (from $299.99)
  - Effective price range: ~$2 individual book to ~$2,775 premium scholarly collection (street prices $130–$400 on sale).
- **Subscriptions:** Bible Study Packs / Starter Pack ~$2.99–$5.99/mo (5 translation-keyed packs: NIV, ESV, NKJV, CSB, NLT); Commentary Select ~$8.99–$19.99/mo. 14-day free trial.

Note: the competitor brief flagged a "premium collection ~$700" and "books from ~$20" — confirmed in shape: à la carte books cluster $10–$40, with high-end commentary collections having list prices in the $560–$2,775 band (sale-discounted to the $130–$400 range). No single product was found at exactly $700; the brief figure is consistent with this band as an estimate.

## Transformation(s)
- dominant frame: get more out of your time in Scripture / become better-equipped to read and study God's Word — a competence-and-depth outcome, not a presence/focus outcome
- secondary: achieve personal study goals (habit / consistency); grow in your faith (Study Pack copy)
- evidence: "Striving to inspire and equip people like you to read and study God's Word"; "get more out of your Bible study time"; "Apply God's Word to your life with the right tools and training"; "Together, the right tools and training will give you everything you need for growing in your faith."

## Niche
- niche: general / not specified — copy targets "people like you," ordinary Christian readers, with no demographic, denominational, or role segmentation on the homepage
- targeted sub-niche: the self-directed lay Bible student — a churchgoer who wants to study seriously but is NOT a credentialed scholar; the brand explicitly de-risks the depth ("Leave the stress of choosing to us"; "basic original language study"). Positioned between free apps (YouVersion) and scholar-tier software (Logos). The high-end commentary collections (BDAG/HALOT, Old & New Testament Library) imply a thin scholar/pastor sub-segment is also served by the store, but marketing copy does not lead with it.
- evidence: "inspire and equip people like you to read and study God's Word"; "You'll receive guided trainings, teaching you to get the most out of every Bible study tool in your pack"; "hand-selected study Bibles, maps, and more" — curation language signals a non-expert buyer

## Mechanism / UM
- mechanism(s) — how/why it works: a Resource Guide that surfaces relevant commentaries/maps/dictionaries for the passage you are reading; curated translation-keyed "Study Packs" that bundle the right tools so the user does not have to assemble a study library themselves; guided trainings that teach the user how to use each tool; cross-device sync so study work persists everywhere
- problem-mechanism (causal story, may be shared): implicit — serious Bible study requires the right reference tools and the know-how to use them, and the cost/complexity of assembling and learning those tools is a barrier ("Leave the stress of choosing to us"). Not a uniquely-positioned cause.
- UM: none — every mechanism (sync, resource guide, curated bundles, audio) is a recognized category capability. The closest to a Feature-UM is the curated "Study Pack + guided training" bundle, but it is positioned as convenience, not as a unique mechanism that competitors lack.

# Competitive Set — Dumb Device Market

**Market:** Loose-niche buyers purchasing stripped-down/specialist utility tech or
distraction-blocking tools · transformation = **more control** over their
relationship with technology (digital minimalism / unplugging / agency — NOT
productivity-productivity).
**Fixed product:** $900 foldable, programmable e-ink tablet.
**Competitive set = anything the buyer purchases to take more control over their
tech or attention.** Six strata. Per stratum: ranked by market relevance +
size/scale. ★ = must-analyze.

Niche is LOOSE — sub-niches are READ from each brand's copy, not pre-assumed. The
"targeted sub-niche + verbatim evidence" column is the primary deliverable.

Compiled by FINDER agent · 2026-05-23. Roster only — no in-depth analysis.

## Loose-niche question — which sub-niches recur most?

Reading sub-niches as a frequency table across the roster:

1. **Digital minimalists / "intentional tech" buyers** — recurs in every stratum
   except #6. The clearest universal: Light Phone ("going Light"), Punkt
   ("Digital Minimalist Pioneers"), Mudita ("mindful living"), Brick ("Take Back
   Your Time"), Daylight ("calm-tech"). Vocabulary varies; the cluster is one
   thing.
2. **Compulsive scrollers / phone-addicted** — recurs heavily in software (one
   sec, ScreenZen, Unpluq, Opal, Freedom) and in physical blockers (Brick).
   This is the **mass-market** version of #1 — much larger TAM, less
   identity-loaded.
3. **Parents / families** — recurs in dumb phones (Wisephone), lockboxes (Yondr
   Home Tray, kSafe, Brick), and OS defaults (Apple Screen Time, Family Link).
   Strong cross-stratum recurrence — distinct buyer role (parent buys for self
   AND for kid).
4. **Writers / creative knowledge workers** — owns Stratum 2 entirely (Freewrite
   product line) and shows up in Stratum 3 e-ink (Supernote "For Those Who
   Write", Freewrite Wordrunner) and Stratum 5 (Cold Turkey Writer, Freedom for
   creators).
5. **Students** — recurs in software blockers (Forest, Opal, Cold Turkey,
   Freedom), Yondr B2B (schools), and lockboxes (Brick — students named on the
   page). Lives at the intersection of "more control" and productivity; bleeds
   toward the Students-market scan.
6. **ADHD-identifying adults** — under-targeted on supply side relative to
   demand. Freedom cites it in reviews ("Must-have for ADHD"), Opal lists it as
   a segment. **No brand in this roster leads with ADHD** — early signal of an
   underserved sub-niche.

**Top 3 recurring sub-niches across the roster: (1) digital minimalists /
intentional-tech, (2) compulsive scrollers, (3) parents/families.** All three
are real and cross-stratum. The control transformation reads cleanly across (1)
and (2); parents are a distinct buyer-role layer that crosses all three.

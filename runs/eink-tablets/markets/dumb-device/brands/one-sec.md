## Mechanism / UM
- mechanism(s) — how/why it works: **Friction, not block.** When the user opens a configured app (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube, etc.), one sec injects a forced pause — typically a deep-breath animation — before the app loads. The pause does two things: (a) interrupts the unconscious habit loop in the moment, giving the user a window to abandon the open; (b) over repetition, *conditions aversion* — the user starts to associate opening the app with the friction, "rewiring" the urge. Variants (mirror, rotate, type, 4-7-8 breathing) all serve the same friction principle.
- problem-mechanism (causal story, may be shared): The phone / addictive apps pull users into unconscious, compulsive scrolling that they don't actually want. Shared across the entire digital-minimalism + screen-time category (Light Phone, Brick, Opal, Freedom, Apple Screen Time all tell some version). NOT uniquely owned.
- UM: **Product-UM — "friction, not blocking."** This is genuinely distinctive *response-mechanism* positioning within the software-blocker stratum. Direct contrast vs. the category default (hard blockers like Freedom, Cold Turkey, ScreenZen). The site explicitly leans on this via the testimonial "Strict blockers never worked: I just turned them off. The forced pause is critical." Paired with the "Rewire your brain to hate scrolling" claim, the breath-interrupt isn't a feature — it's positioned as a recategorization of what a screen-time tool should *do* (interrupt and condition vs. block and bypass). Distinct from the **problem-mechanism** (which is shared) — one sec's UM is on the *solution / response* side, not the cause side. Backed by PNAS 2023 publication + Max-Planck/Heidelberg collaboration (scientific moat reinforces the UM as "the friction approach that actually works").

Trustpilot/justuseapp surfaces a vocal minority complaining about (a) aggressive paywall / "asks you to upgrade to pro instead of giving you the deep breath," (b) features moved behind paywall ("was great a few months ago, not worth it now"), (c) bugs.

## Notes / gaps
- **Meta Ad Library unresolved** after 2 attempts — recorded as page-unresolved per framework. The absence is itself data: one sec does not appear to run Meta paid acquisition. Distribution is organic (press, App Store features, influencer endorsement, PNAS science PR). Contrast to Hallow (~1,300 active ads) — entirely different growth model.
- one sec has the **most differentiated mechanism positioning in the software-blocker stratum**. "Friction, not block" is a real Product-UM, validated by both the testimonial frame on-site ("strict blockers never worked") and the PNAS publication. This is rare in the dumb-device market — most competitors share the same problem-mechanism story (phone is the distraction) and offer the same response (block it). one sec differentiates on the *response side*.
- **Caution on conflation:** the problem-mechanism (phone-as-distraction) is shared and is NOT one sec's UM. The UM is the *solution-side positioning* (friction over blocking). These are separate layers and must not be merged.
- **Relevance to our e-ink tablet:** one sec sells the same transformation (more control / less compulsive scrolling) at a $0-$100 price point as a software layer ON the phone the user already owns. Our $900 hardware sells the same transformation as a *device swap*. one sec validates that the transformation has a paying audience and that "friction not deprivation" is a winning response-mechanism — both are useful priors. However, one sec also caps the willingness-to-pay ceiling for buyers who'd settle for software: anyone for whom a $20/yr app works is not a $900 tablet buyer. The opening for our device is the segment for whom one sec's friction is insufficient (i.e. the buyer still bypasses it on the phone) and who needs a hardware-enforced second device.
- Niche field stays general — copy makes no identity claim. Sub-niche reads as compulsive-scrollers with a productivity-adjacent halo via influencer choices.

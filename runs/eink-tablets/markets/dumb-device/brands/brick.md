## Product
Brick — a small magnetic NFC tile ("the ultimate solution to reclaim your focus, productivity, and everyday presence"). Hardware + free companion app (iOS 17+ / Android 12+). User selects which apps/notifications to block in the app, then physically taps the phone to the Brick tile to toggle blocked / unblocked. Modes are customizable; scheduling supported; Strict Mode prevents deleting the app or working around the block; 5 included emergency unbricks.
Pricing model — one-time hardware purchase, no subscription:
- $59.00 single Brick
- Free shipping on orders of 2+ units
- 20% student discount via Student Beans (one ad explicitly routes to studentbeans.com)
- 30-day money-back guarantee
- HSA / FSA eligible
- "One Brick works with multiple phones; one phone works with multiple Bricks"

## Transformation(s)
- dominant frame: Take back control over your time and attention from your phone — buyer moves from a state of being controlled by their phone (scrolling, interruptions, missing the moment) to a state of being in control and present in their own life.
- secondary: (1) Focus / productivity ("reclaim your focus, productivity, and everyday presence"); (2) Be present with family / loved ones ("Be Present for What Matters"); (3) Better sleep / better mornings (review-driven, e.g. "racking up embarrassing amounts of screen time, especially in the morning… and at night when I needed to be going to bed"); (4) Reduce social-media addiction (creator ad: "cut my screentime from 8hrs to 2hrs").
- evidence: Hero — "Take Back Your Time"; "Brick is the physical device that blocks distracting apps from your phone"; "Turn your phone back into a tool that supports your goals"; "One device. Every part of your day, protected." Ad-creative headline reused 100+ times: "Your Phone, Minus the Distractions." Page name itself = "Brick - Take Back Your Time."

## Mechanism / UM
- mechanism(s) — how/why it works: Physical friction. The NFC tile is left in another room / on the fridge / at home, so unblocking requires standing up and walking to the tile. The "key" to your distractions is physically separated from your phone — pattern-interrupts the automatic reach-for-phone reflex and forces a deliberate pause. Customizable per-mode blocking lets the user keep essentials (Uber, Venmo, Maps) while killing the dopamine apps, removing the "but I need my phone" objection that defeats dumbphone / Light-Phone alternatives.
- problem-mechanism (causal story, may be shared): "Every one of these [Screen Time Limits, Deleting Apps, Grayscale Mode] lives inside your phone, making them easy to bypass." The cause of failed self-regulation is that the lever lives on the same device as the addiction. SHARED across the digital-minimalism category — Light Phone, Daylight, Opal, Freedom and Brick all tell some version of "the phone is the source of distraction" — but Brick sharpens it to a sub-claim: "the *fix* lives on the same device, so the fix fails." That sub-claim is more pointed than the generic shared story.
- UM: Product-UM (borderline) — "The solution isn't another app — it's a physical barrier." This is positioned as uniquely Brick: a hardware solution that explicitly disqualifies the entire software-blocker / OS-default category by name. Verbatim copy: "With other solutions, the 'key' to your distractions is always within reach. With Brick, you physically leave that key behind." The buyer has to mentally move the product into a new category (a *physical* anti-distraction object, not "another screen time app"). That clears the Product-UM bar in the framework. Note: this UM is shared at the *stratum* level with kSafe (physical safe) and Yondr (magnetic pouch); within the dumb-device market it is *not* unique to Brick alone, but within the broader phone-distraction category dominated by software it functions as a Product-UM. Score it Product-UM at the market-vs-software level; downgrade to "feature-UM / stratum-shared" if compared only against kSafe / Yondr.

## Sophistication
- market sophistication stage this competitor plays at: Stage 3-4 of the digital-minimalism / phone-distraction market. Multiple Product UMs already exist in the category (Light Phone = minimalist phone; Daylight = minimalist e-ink device; Opal / Freedom = software blocker; kSafe = physical safe; Yondr = magnetic pouch). Brick competes on (a) an alternative Product UM ("NFC tile" vs. "safe / pouch / phone-replacement"), (b) tight Feature UMs (NFC tap, customizable modes, Strict Mode, multi-device pairing) that disqualify both software and other hardware blockers, (c) angle layering (parent / student / creator / addict segmentation), (d) value model (no subscription, HSA / FSA, student discount, 30-day guarantee), and (e) growing prestige-press trust. That layered stack is Stage-3/4 behavior. Brick does not yet need to play Stage-5 (no celebrity / no Super Bowl) but is approaching the prestige-press threshold.

## Notes / gaps
- The "physical barrier, not another app" Product-UM is the sharpest differentiator Brick owns versus the software-blocker stratum (Opal / Freedom / One Sec / Forest). Inside the physical-blocker stratum (kSafe / Yondr-consumer / Unpluq), Brick's specific Feature-UM stack is: NFC-tap + customizable per-mode blocking + cross-platform (iOS + Android) + magnet-mountable form factor + no subscription. kSafe is a timer-locked container (you put the phone *inside*); Yondr is a magnetic pouch (same — phone goes in, can't come out); Unpluq is the closest analog (an NFC dongle), but Brick has more reviews and far more marketing share-of-voice. Inside the stratum, Brick's UM is closer to Feature-UM territory.
- The "Created by two young guys" founder story is repeated in ~80% of brand-direct ads — it's their dominant trust hook, working as both belonging (relatable normal guys) and anti-establishment (vs. Big Tech). Notable that this scales as the brand grows; eventually it'll feel incongruent with 35K+ reviews and prestige press.
- ADHD is the largest unclaimed sub-niche — Brick is heavily recommended for ADHD adults by third parties, but the brand does not name ADHD anywhere in on-site copy or in the ads I sampled. Likely deliberate: naming ADHD could narrow the niche or invoke FDA/medical-claim risk. Open opening for a competitor (e.g. our e-ink tablet) to claim the ADHD positioning directly.
- HSA / FSA eligibility is a high-value buyer-friction feature, not a claim — it dramatically lowers effective price for the wellness-framed sub-segment but is not visible in most ads (only on the home page). Under-leveraged.
- Revenue range ($6.7M – $12M ARR) is low confidence. Two third-party sources conflict (GetLatka "bootstrapped, never raised" vs. PitchBook "$5M raised"). The 9-person team / no-retail / Meta-paid-only pattern is consistent with a mid-DTC scale.
- Relevance to our e-ink tablet ($900 foldable programmable e-ink): Brick validates the *mechanism* (physical friction beats software self-regulation) at a 15× lower price point. The e-ink tablet's edge is not "is the physical-barrier idea viable" (Brick proves yes) — it's "is the buyer willing to pay 15× more for a richer mechanism (a screen they actually *use* during the unplugged time, vs. just locking apps away)." Brick targets the moment the phone goes *away*; the e-ink tablet targets what the user does *instead* of the phone. Adjacent transformation, different price ceiling, potentially complementary rather than competitive.

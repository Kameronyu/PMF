# Students Market — Market Profile

Aggregated from 13 per-competitor records · `runs/eink-tablets/markets/students/brands/`
(+ 4 Faith records reused for e-ink incumbents) · compiled 2026-05-23.
Market = students (K-12 + college, ONE market) × transformation = better grades / academic
performance via focus. Fixed product = $900 foldable, programmable e-ink tablet.

**This is an evidence dossier. No scoring, no weighting — assembly only.**

## Per-cell aggregation

### Cell A — School-issued workspace (Chromebook, Surface, iPad-in-Education; n=3)

    Android + Steam (per chromebook.md). iPad-in-Education's Classroom + Shared iPad is a real

## Cross-cell synthesis

### Buyer-side synthesis (primary deliverable for this market)

Students is **structurally buyer ≠ user, mixed across three buyer types**. Each cell's buyer
dominance + wallet realism for $900:

- **Cell A — School-buyer (K-12 districts).** Dominated by Chromebook (~52–60% US K-12 shipments,
  93% of districts purchased 2025–26 per chromebook.md). iPad-in-Education ~13% overall device
  share / ~52% of K-12 *tablet* slice (per ipad.md). Surface holds Windows minority and lost its
  SE bottom-tier. **Spend pattern:** district RFP / ESSER (2020–2024) / now 68% locally funded;
  $200–$400/unit + per-device management license. **Where $900 lands:** structurally
  uncompetitive. We cannot win the K-12 institutional bid at $900 + no Google Admin Console
  integration + no MDM story. Don't try.
- **Cell B — Parent-buyer for K-8 / early high school.** Dominated by iPad consumer (Apple
  gravity + parent default trust); Fire HD Kids at the cheap end (per roster, not analyzed);
  **Daylight Kids @ $729 + ESA-funded payment path is the only premium-e-ink validation in this
  buyer type.** Gabb / Bark Phone / Wisephone / Pinwheel / Troomi compete for the parent's
  *distraction-removal device* dollar on the phone form factor (Cell E). **Spend pattern:**
  birthday / Christmas / back-to-school / "first device" rite of passage; subscription LTV
  (Gabb/Bark/Wisephone) or one-shot $700–$799 (Daylight Kids, Light Phone). **Where $900 lands:**
  defensible *only* with Daylight Kids' playbook — values + health-stakes + ESA-funded payment
  path + named-authority endorsement. Daylight Kids has proven a parent will pay $729 for an
  e-ink learning device when the pitch is "save my kid from iPad" + state-funded financing. We
  are $171 above that anchor.
- **Cell C — Student-self-buyer (college, often parent-financed).** Dominated by MacBook Air
  (~45% top US universities per macbook-air.md). iPad Air / iPad Pro for all-in-one. e-ink
  (Cell D) is the focus-seeking power-user sub-segment. **Spend pattern:** back-to-school
  (Jun–Sep window), incoming-freshman gift, self-upgrade. **Average college-student electronics
  spend = $359.49 per student / $22.8B total (NRF 2025); 70% of students prioritize price under
  $1,000; 96% laptop ownership; 45% use MacBooks** (NRF; electroiq 2026). **Where $900 lands:**
  defensible in college student-self / parent-for-student wallet — $999 MacBook Air anchors the
  reference, $670 effective iPad Air 13" + BTS-free-Pencil bundle is the substitute floor. $900
  is a believable college price; transformation differentiation has to do the work because
  capability (Apple) and price (Boox $323 net edu) bracket us on either side.

**Verdict — the same product faces three different buyer rationales depending on segment.** The
$900 e-ink tablet **cannot** win K-12 school procurement (Cell A is locked at $200–$400 by
structural Chromebook dominance). It **can** win parent-of-K-8 if it borrows Daylight Kids'
playbook (values + ESA + endorsement). It **can** win college student-self if it differentiates
on a transformation Apple structurally won't make (focus → grades). The market is not one
homogeneous buyer — it is three different buyers whose only common ground is "they buy stuff for
students."

### Differentiation whitespace

**The single clearest whitespace, with verbatim verification: an academic-performance / "better
grades via focus" outcome claim. Zero of 13 competitors makes it.**

Within-cell counts:

- Cell A (Chromebook, Surface, iPad-in-Education): **0/3** make a grade / test-score / academic-
  outcome claim. iPad's "It aces testing standards, too" refers to *iPad's app compatibility with
  standardized testing apps*, not test-score gains (per ipad.md). Chromebook explicitly null on
  grades (per chromebook.md). Surface "students achieve their potential" is aspirational filler.
- Cell B: **0/2.** Daylight Kids' "smarter children" header is the closest — proof points stop
  at curated apps + focus + bedtime (per daylight.md delta). iPad K-12 = creative engagement, not
  measurable academic gain.
- Cell C: **0/3.** Achievement verbs ("ace," "crush," "tear through" — Apple) are mood, not
  claim. "Copilot helps you stay productive and focused" (Surface) is AI capability, not academic
  outcome.
- Cell D: **0/5.** reMarkable's NHH-pilot stats ("70% found it easier to focus," "20% more focus,"
  "17% sharper memory" per remarkable.md delta) — closest in the market, but framing **stops at
  focus / less stress and never closes the loop to grades.** Boox: "distraction-free study" +
  "deep flow state" but no academic-outcome claim (per boox.md). Kindle Scribe / Supernote /
  Daylight DC-1: silent on student-academic-performance entirely.
- Cell E: **0/4.** Gabb founder PR mentions "academic achievements jeopardized" but **never in
  product copy or ads** (per gabb.md). Bark Phone makes zero academic claims (per bark-phone.md).
  Wisephone's "God-given talents" is values-not-grades. Light Phone silent.

**Headline finding: 0/13. No competitor in any cell, in any buyer type, in any price band, makes
a "better grades" claim. reMarkable owns the upstream half of the chain (focus / less distraction
/ less mental strain) and stops short of the downstream half (grades / academic outcome) — the
most explicit gap in the dataset.** The grades-outcome promise is open positioning across every
stratum.

# Competitive Set — Students Market

**Market:** Students (K-12 + college, ONE market — buyer-side mixed) · transformation = better grades / academic performance via focus.
**Fixed product:** $900 foldable, programmable e-ink tablet.
**Competitive set = every tech-heavy device a parent / student / school actually purchases for a student workspace.** Non-tech substitutes (paper planners, notebooks) excluded per brief. Five strata. Ranked by actual current sales / share. ★ = must-analyze.

Compiled by FINDER agent · 2026-05-23. Roster only — no in-depth analysis.

This is the structural top of the competitive set — more units than every other stratum combined.

## Summary

- **Roster counts:** Stratum 1 = 6 · Stratum 2 = 5 · Stratum 3 = 5 · Stratum 4 = 6 · Stratum 5 = 5 = **27 entries**, **13 must-analyze** (4 of which are Faith-record deltas, not fresh analyses).
- **Buyer-dominance answer (plain):** Parent dominates Stratum 4 + 5 (kid-safe hardware, monitoring software) and the Fire HD / iPad-for-elementary segment. School dominates K-12 via Chromebook. Student-self dominates the college laptop purchase (MacBook Air > Windows premium) and *all* of e-ink. The same product faces three different buyer rationales depending on the segment it lands in — important structural feature of this market.
- **Surprises / ambiguity worth flagging at the human checkpoint:**
  1. **Chromebook is structurally enormous** (~52% of US K-12 shipments, 93% district purchase rate) but priced $150–500 — the bottom of the market by 2x+ vs. the $900 device. The $900 e-ink tablet *cannot* compete with a Chromebook on price for the K-12 issued-device job; the relevant competitive set for parent/student-self buyers is iPad + MacBook Air + e-ink, not Chromebook. Worth deciding now whether to even bother analyzing Chromebook deeply (recommend: yes, but tagged as "incumbent-context, not a head-to-head rival").
  2. **Bark Phone + Gabb are the cleanest analog to this product's transformation** — both are sold on "distraction is the problem, hardware is the solution." Same Problem-mechanism the brief hypothesizes. They sit at $200 hardware + monthly plan, not $900 one-shot — but the *narrative* overlap is the closest in the entire competitive set (closer than even Daylight). Flag for analyzer attention.
  3. **No e-ink player markets to students** — same null finding as the Faith run. The closest is Boox publishing the Note Air with academic-use blog posts; reMarkable has student-testimonial videos but no targeted student-niche campaign. Whitespace finding is structurally identical to Faith, which is itself a signal.
  4. **The "students-self for focus" sub-segment skews college, not K-12** — the brief locks "one market, don't bifurcate," but the buyer evidence is going to come back split. K-12 = parent or school decides; college = student decides for self with focus framing. Worth flagging to the analyzer agents that the buyer split correlates with K-12 vs. college, even though we're treating the market as one.
  5. **Light Phone / Wisephone overlap with Dumb-Device** — dual-listed here because parents do buy them for teens. Aggregator should coordinate with the Dumb-Device scan to avoid double-analysis.

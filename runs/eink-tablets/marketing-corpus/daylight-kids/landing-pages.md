Q: "What's the difference between the Kids Version and the original DC-1?"
A: "The Kids Version has the same hardware as the original DC-1, but with family-focused software and accessories. It features a kid-friendly interface, curated apps, and parental controls to block mature content, trackers, and social media. It also comes with a protective Kids Case and a Pencil-style stylus."

Q: "How is the Daylight less stimulating and less addicting than an iPad?"
A: "iPads have hyperstimulating colors, flickering backlights, and blue light that disrupts sleep. Their interface uses dopamine-driven design with constant notifications to maximize screen time. On the other hand, Daylight's paper-like display is monochrome, blue-light-free, and flicker-free, supporting natural circadian rhythms. Our software is built for focus, not engagement—no pop-ups or vibrations."

Q: "Can my child play games and watch YouTube kids on the Daylight?"
A: "Yes, you can download games and media apps such as YouTube Kids from the Google Play Store. While the Daylight can run them, the grayscale paper-like display is intentionally designed to make games and media less stimulating and addictive than on a typical color screen. We have also curated a selection of apps for children that are entertaining and educational."

Q: "Is the Daylight durable enough for kids?"

## LP 8 — Blog: Screens and the Brain
URL: https://kids.daylightcomputer.com/blog/screens-and-the-brain
Pulled: 2026-05-23

Title: "Screens and the Brain"
Author: Janice Dietrich
Date: October 10, 2025
Subtitle: "A Mom's Look at the Battle for Our Attention"

Opening:
"As a homeschooling mom of three (ages 6, 3, and 1) who works from home on a screen, I didn't want to believe or accept the effect that screens are having on our brains. After all, I do need to work, and allowing my children to have occasional screen time saves my sanity, especially on hard days. But I can't run from the fact that our attention is under attack."

"Dings, infinite scrolls, and banner notifications are more than a momentary distraction—they are persuasive design techniques based in behavioral psychology to maximize engagement time. This can:"
- "Reshape and rewire the brain: Heavy digital use is associated with changes in attention networks, reward systems, and prefrontal cortex development."
- "Limit our attention spans: Research shows increased digital media use correlates with reduced sustained attention spans."
- "Hijack our focus: Every notification, email check, or social media scroll creates 'attention residue'—part of your mind remains stuck on the previous task."
- "Impair our ability to form and recall memories: Digital overuse affects working memory and can lead to 'digital amnesia' when we rely on our devices rather than remembering."

"This happens to adults at work, students in school, and children at home."

# Daylight Computer — Phase 0 Per-Brand Record

- analyzed_at: 2026-05-20
- brand: Daylight Computer Co.
- official_url(s): https://daylightcomputer.com/ , https://daylightcomputer.com/product , https://buy.daylightcomputer.com/products/daylight-tablet , https://kids.daylightcomputer.com
- panel_type: paper-like reflective LCD — Daylight's proprietary "Live Paper" display. NOT true E Ink electrophoretic. It is a transmissive/reflective monochrome LCD running at 60Hz/60fps with an optional amber backlight and DC dimming (no PWM). Competes directly in the paper-feel / eye-care / focus market against true E Ink devices (Boox, Kindle, reMarkable) but technically is an RLCD-class panel, not electrophoretic.
- sources_used: official site (home + product pages), Meta Ad Library dump (2 runs), Wikipedia, PitchBook/Crunchbase/Mercury (funding), Trustpilot, multiple independent reviews (Liliputing, max.pm, Adventure.com, GizmoDrift, eReadersForum, No Kill Switch, Hacker News), Finesse Group PR case study

## Product
- DC-1 (single model). 10.5" Live Paper display, 1600×1200, 190dpi, 60fps. MediaTek Helio G99, 8GB RAM, 128GB storage, 8000mAh battery, 1.2 lbs / 550g. Wacom EMR passive stylus support, optional Pure Amber backlight w/ DC dimming. Android 13; Sol:OS (custom layer, "coming soon" at time of analysis). No cameras.
- Price: USD $729 (all-time-low discount seen at $649). Single SKU; first run of 5,000 units sold out in 2024, periodically restocked.
- Adjacent product line: "Daylight Kids" — same DC-1 hardware repositioned with curated kid software / parental controls, marketed on kids.daylightcomputer.com.

Heavily coded to the Huberman-adjacent light/circadian-health community (amber backlight, "no blue light," "wellbeing," nature framing). ## Mechanism / UM
- mechanism(s) referenced: "Live Paper" display (60Hz reflective monochrome LCD); zero blue light / Pure Amber backlight; DC dimming, no PWM, flicker-free; sunlight-readable; grayscale = low stimulation.
- UM type played: primarily **Problem UM** + **Product UM** combined.
  - Problem UM: backlit, blue-light, flickering screens wreck focus, sleep, eye health, and (for kids) brain development — "utility based rather than dopamine based" reframes the whole device category around the cause (dopamine/addiction).
  - Product UM: "Live Paper" — a named proprietary display positioned as the solution (paper-like, no blue light, no flicker, usable outside).
  - Feature UM secondary: "world's first full-speed paper-like display" / 60fps — upgrades the recognized e-paper category on a refresh-rate feature vs. slow E Ink.

## Sophistication
- market sophistication stage this brand plays at: **Stage 3, leaning Stage 1/2 within its sub-niche.** The broad e-paper/paper-feel category already has a dominant Product UM (E Ink), so Daylight must offer an alternative — that's Stage 3. But it does it by effectively introducing a *new* product mechanism ("Live Paper," 60fps reflective LCD) and pairing it with a heavy Problem-UM reframe ("dopamine vs utility," "blue light wrecks your health/your kid's brain"), plus founder authority and a values-tribe — i.e. it behaves like Stage 1/2 within the "healthy computer" niche it is trying to create. It is NOT competing on Stage 4 spec one-upmanship.

## Reviews / VOC quick-read
- review volume + rough sentiment: low formal volume (~4 Trustpilot), but substantial qualitative coverage from tech press and independent reviewers/YouTubers. Sentiment is mixed-positive: the display and stylus draw genuine praise; price, software maturity, contrast, and being a single-purpose monochrome device draw criticism.
- brand loyalty signal: strong mission/identity pull — buyers self-select into the "humane tech / caring computer / digital minimalism" ethos and the founder's vision. Loyalty is values-driven, not feature-driven. But the install base is small (5,000-unit first run) so loyalty is concentrated among early adopters.
- hidden use cases / notable likes & dislikes:
  - Likes: "eye comfort is genuinely transformative" for heavy screen users; 60fps "wow effect" vs slow E Ink; best-in-class Android stylus (low latency, good palm rejection); amber backlight + outdoor readability.
  - Dislikes: $729 for a monochrome, camera-less tablet on a budget Helio G99 chip that "chokes on demanding apps"; black not black enough — contrast worse than a Kindle Paperwhite; software/Sol:OS half-baked; ordering & customer-service complaints; doesn't fully replace any single existing device (users "brought more gadgets, not fewer"). Direct competitive knock: "Kindle Scribe does reading better for $230 less; Boox Note Air5 C does Android apps better for $200 less."

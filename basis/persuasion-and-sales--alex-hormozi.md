## 3A Reframing Framework (Acknowledge, Associate, Ask) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

The 3A framework is the skill that separates the best closers from the rest. When a prospect says anything but yes: (1) Acknowledge what they said by restating it, (2) Associate their question with behavior of successful customers, (3) Ask a question that moves toward the close. This maintains rapport while asking for the sale more times.

- Reframing = 1-3 sentences after a prospect says anything but yes that increases close likelihood
- Acknowledge: restate what they said (builds rapport, buys time to think)
- Associate: connect their question to the behavior of your best customers
- Ask: ask a question that moves toward the close
- Person asking questions controls the conversation
- Best closers ask for the sale the most times without decreasing rapport
- Never ask 'do you have any questions' — it invites objections

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 100+ salespeople across portfolio
- Works from $100 to $1M+ sales

> "The person asking the questions is the one who's in control"
> "You close sales by making the prospect right"

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Never ask 'do you have any questions' — it invites objections

## AB Offer (Anchor-Core-Downsell) Strategy (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Structure three offer tiers — a high anchor, a core offer, and a downsell — to use contrast pricing psychology. The high anchor makes the core offer appear as a steal; the low downsell makes the core offer the obvious rational choice.

- Anchor offer ($25,000 with zero commission) sets a high reference price
- Core offer ($5,800 with commission) appears as a bargain relative to anchor
- Downsell ($2,000-$3,000 DIY package) makes core offer look like obvious choice to avoid DIY risk
- Three tiers create two contrast effects simultaneously, both pushing prospects toward the core offer

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Anchor offer: $25,000 flat, zero commission
- Core offer: $5,800 upfront plus performance commission
- Downsell: $2,000-$3,000 DIY package
- 'For only $2,800 more, I can ensure it's done right' — anchoring against downsell

> "The high anchor makes the core offer appear as a steal. The low down-sell option makes the core offer look like the obvious choice."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Downsell ($2,000-$3,000 DIY package) makes core offer look like obvious choice to avoid DIY risk

## Anchoring and Increment Anchoring (Source: Alex Hormozi)

The first number set in a negotiation becomes the anchor. Daniel Kahneman's Nobel Prize research shows people give excessive weight to initial information and make insufficient adjustments. Anchor as high as possible - 'get the gasp.' Also anchor the increments of movement: if they move $3M toward you, move $250K toward them to signal you won't budge much.

- First number stated becomes the anchor for the entire negotiation
- Kahneman: people give excessive weight to initial information and adjust insufficiently
- Anchor high to shift all subsequent thinking to higher increments
- Also anchor movement increments: move small amounts to signal inflexibility
- Pro tip: if you accept quickly, tell them 'I wouldn't have done it for a penny more/less' to build goodwill
- Get them bidding against themselves by asking what fixing the problem would cost them

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- House listed at $25M, dropped to $20M. Alex offered $15M. Counter: $17M. Alex countered $15.25M (moved $250K vs their $3M move)
- $4M in furniture used as additional negotiation variable

> "Daniel Kahneman, a Nobel Prize winner, figured out that people give excessive weight to the initial information and make insufficient adjustments from that starting point."
> "If you say 'I'll do this work for $100,000' and someone was thinking $10,000, their increments now become $20,000 or higher."

## Authority Objection Framework (Permission vs Support) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

When someone says they need to talk to their partner, isolate the objection because the partner is not there and cannot actually object. The real issue is asking for permission instead of support. Reframe: 'I'm not asking your permission, I'm asking for your support.'

- Ask what they think the partner wouldn't like - then overcome that specific thing
- If rules were reversed, would they support their partner's dream
- The real problem is asking permission instead of support
- A 3-day guarantee helps nudge people over when partner concern is genuine

> "The real problem is that you're asking for permission instead of support. It is your life, not theirs."

## Avoidance Objection Frameworks (Past, Present, Future) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

When all external reasons are exhausted and someone says 'I need to think about it,' handle through three timeframes. Past: don't let bad experiences burn you twice, this isn't a fast decision. Present: the Rocking Chair Close and Three Decision Questions. Future: consider the options logically.

- Past: bad experiences burn you twice if they prevent good investments
- Present: people don't need time, they need information - you are the information source
- The Rocking Chair Close: you won't sit and deliberate, you'll go back to life and forget
- Three Questions: do you believe the product works, do you trust me, do you think it'll work for you
- Future: only one option guarantees failure - walking out the door
- Etymology of decide: Latin 'dcad' means to cut off/kill off - which future are we killing

> "People think they need more time to make decisions. They assume they will get more information in that time. But if you are the source of the information, then time does not help them."
> "The etymology of the word comes from Latin - dcad - which means to cut off, to kill off. Which future are we killing off today?"

**Common Pitfalls:**

## BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

From Harvard Business School: your best alternative to the current deal is the greatest source of psychological power in negotiations. Negotiators who know their alternatives set higher aspirations, make more aggressive first offers, and negotiate better outcomes. All preparation happens before you sit at the table. You only want to sell when you don't want to sell.

- BATNA = your best option if this deal falls through
- Provides psychological power, confidence, and higher anchoring
- London Business School: negotiators with strong BATNAs set higher aspirations and get better outcomes
- For employees: negotiate when you already have another offer
- For vendors: get multiple bids before deciding; learn terms from each to use in future negotiations
- For partnerships: have multiple potential deals in play
- If you sit down needing the deal with no alternatives, no tactics will save you
- You only want to sell when you don't want to sell; buy when you don't want to buy

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Alex negotiating home purchase: best alternative is doing nothing and enjoying current home
- When selling Gym Launch and Prestige Labs: could just keep them making money

> "You only want to sell when you don't want to sell. You want to buy when you don't want to buy because you have something else."
> "You get what you negotiate, not what you deserve."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- You only want to sell when you don't want to sell; buy when you don't want to buy

## Bonuses as Objection Killers (Stack Building) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Instead of discounting, create bonuses that overcome specific objections. Each time a prospect presents a unique obstacle (travel, budget, time), create a solution (travel meal plan, budget grocery guide, at-home workout guide). Template these solutions and add them to your 'stack' of bonuses. In one-on-one selling, deploy specific bonuses only when the matching objection comes up.

- Never discount core product -- add bonuses instead
- Each customer objection becomes a one-time bonus creation effort used forever after
- Build a library of bonuses: travel meal plan, budget grocery guide, shake guide, at-home workout guide
- In 1:1 selling, match the specific bonus to the specific objection as it arises
- If someone insists on a discount, change the terms: 'Bring 3 friends and I'll give you $100 off'
- Use discounts as bartering tools for advertising (reviews, referrals, social posts)

> "every single thing that people would present with, I would take the one-time effort of making a beautiful solution for them"
> "we don't negotiate with price terrorists"

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Never discount core product -- add bonuses instead

## Chunked-Up High-Level Discovery Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Detailed questions transfer control of the conversation to the prospect and distract from the ROI-driving purpose of the call. - Stay high-level to focus on things that move the needle and tie to biggest ROI
- Detail questions put prospect in control of conversation
- Don't play business consultant on sales calls — you're not doing delivery
- High-level framing maintains closer control and emotional momentum

> "Don't get cute in sales calls. As you get more experienced, you might start playing business consultant or expert, but that's a mistake."

## Criticism vs. Judgment Distinction (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Distinguishing productive self-criticism (identifying discrepancies and learning from them) from self-judgment (attaching moral failure to discrepancies). Criticism enables improvement; judgment paralyzes.

- Criticism = identify discrepancy + ask why = actionable
- Judgment = identify discrepancy + conclude you are bad = destructive
- Example: 'She makes more than me — why?' is criticism. 'She makes more than me therefore I suck' is judgment
- Seeing a competitor's advantage as data to learn from, not as evidence of personal failure

> "There's a difference between criticism and insults. If you criticize yourself you point out a discrepancy... That's criticism. Like I could do better. Judgment is: and because of that you are bad. That's different."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Seeing a competitor's advantage as data to learn from, not as evidence of personal failure

## Diagnostic Sales Pricing by Goal Achievement (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Price services by goal achievement timeline rather than fixed package tiers. Calculate weeks required to hit client goal, multiply by weekly rate, present total as outcome-based investment. Include guarantee: client continues working with you until goal is achieved if they follow protocol.

- Price by goal, not by time package (e.g., 3-month, 6-month)
- Formula: weeks to goal × weekly rate = total price (e.g., 40 lbs ÷ 1 lb/week × $129/week = $5,160)
- Include outcome guarantee: work continues until goal hits if protocol followed
- Converts higher percentage of prospects than fixed timeline packages
- Works for fitness, coaching, and any goal-oriented service

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**

## Discovery Below the Iceberg (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Most sales teams ask surface-level discovery questions ('How much money do you want to make?'). The real money is below the surface — understanding WHY someone wants what they want. Intention-based discovery (why does this matter, who else does it affect, what changes as a result) provides the ammunition to close at the end and determines how you frame the entire pitch.

- Surface-level: 'How much money do you want to make?'
- Below the iceberg: 'Why do you want that? What changes? Who else does it affect? Why does that matter?'
- Different intentions (replace income vs. quit job vs. side hustle) require different pitches
- Deeper discovery = more conviction at close = higher cash collected upfront
- If the salesperson talks more than the prospect, discovery is likely broken

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Close rate improved from 27% to 41% after rescripting discovery

> "The deeper you get the discovery, the more convicted the buyer will be about the solution and the more likely they are to pay upfront as a measure of their conviction."

## Discovery Through Pulling Teeth (Source: Alex Hormozi)

## Five Levels of Competence (Source: Alex Hormozi)

## Five Rules for Ethical Reframing (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Five rules govern ethical use of the 3A reframing framework: (1) prospects believe everything they say and nothing you say, (2) never disagree with a prospect, (3) tell them what their question means using association, (4) use straw men for tough truths, (5) retain childlike curiosity at all times.

- Rule 1: Prospect believes their words, not yours — get them to sell themselves
- Rule 2: Never disagree — you never win a sale by winning an argument
- Rule 3: Tell them what their question means using the three association types
- Rule 4: Use straw men (past customer, earlier conversation, authority figure) for tough truths
- Rule 5: Retain childlike curiosity — say 'huh' to buy time and stay non-combative
- Keep the human number one — seek to understand, not to win

> "A prospect believes almost nothing about what you say and almost everything that they say"
> "It's a dance not a fight — it's seduction not rape"

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Rule 2: Never disagree — you never win a sale by winning an argument

## Five-Step Diagnostic Sales Process (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A five-step consultative sales process that replaces transactional pitching with diagnostic questioning. Salesperson positions as trusted expert, collects customer pain data, anchors on customer-stated goals, and presents product as the bridge to their desired outcome. Used to 10X recurring revenue across a 14-location chain without changing core service delivery.

- Step 1: Pre-sale questionnaire agitates problem by asking pain questions multiple ways (what brought you in, financial cost, 5-year projection)
- Step 2: Collect credit card before presenting any offer, framed as completing customer profile
- Step 3: Identify current state vs. desired state vs. obstacle — use customer's own language
- Step 4: Anticipate and neutralize objections before they arise ('killing zombies'); position as curious expert, not salesperson
- Step 5: Implement and drill until team delivers script automatically without thinking
- Critical rule: If you win the argument in a sale, you lose the sale

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 14-location chain scaled to 32 locations in 18 months
- Recurring revenue share went from 9% to 60% (664% increase)
- Customers on memberships went from ~0% to 30%
- Average visits per customer went from 2 to 4-6 (300-500% increase)
- Monthly revenue from service line went from $220,000 to $250,000+ and growing

> "If you win the argument in a sale, you lose the sale. The only way to win is by being willing to lose being right."
> "The magic happens when you bridge a standardized product with a custom presentation."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Step 4: Anticipate and neutralize objections before they arise ('killing zombies'); position as curious expert, not salesperson

## Framing (Investment vs Cost) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

How you position something determines how it's perceived and negotiated. Frame your offerings as investments with returns, not costs. Frame the other party's offerings as costs, not investments. Support your frame with data showing ROI. Most people don't understand framing and will accept whichever frame is presented to them.

- Frame your side as investment with returns; frame their side as cost/overhead
- Instead of 'this costs $5K,' say 'for a $5K investment, you'll see $15K in savings'
- As employee: frame yourself as investment, not cost center - tie to revenue/returns
- As vendor: frame services as investments with measurable ROI
- Support frames with data (comparable sales, industry benchmarks)
- Example: pool costs $100K but adds $100K to home value = 'the pool is free, you just get to enjoy it'

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Pool example: $100K cost, adds $100K-$200K in home value depending on neighborhood

> "Rather than saying 'Hey, this is going to cost you five grand,' we can say 'For a $5,000 investment, you can see $15,000 in maintenance cost savings.'"

## Guarantee Formula (Source: Alex Hormozi)

The guarantee formula is: 'If you don't achieve X by Y time, I will Z.' X is the promised outcome, Y is the timeline, and Z is the consideration (what you put at stake). The 'or what' gives the guarantee its power -- just saying 'guaranteed' is not enough. The two most effective guarantee types are service-based (continue working until achieved) and unconditional (money back if value not perceived).

- Formula: If you don't achieve X by Y time, I will Z
- The 'or what' (Z) gives the guarantee its power, not just the word 'guarantee'
- Service-based guarantee: continue working with client until goal is achieved
- Unconditional guarantee: money back if value isn't perceived, independent of results
- Team knowing the guarantee exists forces them to level up delivery
- Guarantees can also be sold as a paid add-on (not always free)

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 11 favorite guarantee types detailed in $100M Offers book

> "if you don't achieve X by Y time, I will Z, which is what's the consideration"

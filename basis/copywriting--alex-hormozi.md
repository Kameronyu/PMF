## Ad Copy: Call Out, Pain Hook, Benefit-Driven Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Structure ads with three layers: First, a clear avatar call-out that gets the right people to notice (e.g., '$4M+ restaurant owners'). Second, a pain-based hook that articulates their specific frustrations better than they can. Third, benefit-driven copy that describes the experience of success rather than features. 80% of advertising work is done by the call-out alone.

- Call-out is the most important element: compress ideal avatar qualifications into one line
- 80% of advertising work is done by calling out the right prospect
- Pain-based hooks are super compliant and more effective than promises
- Describe moments and experiences, not features ('create drinks that create regulars' vs. 'data-driven menus')
- Use the What/Who/When framework to generate copy variations from the value equation
- Translate process improvements into the specific moments customers experience the benefit

> "If you can articulate the pain of a prospect more accurately to themselves than they can articulate it, they will believe that you can help them almost better than any promise that you can make them."
> "80% of the work of advertising is actually done by the call-out."

## Always Call Out Who You're Looking For (Audience Specificity) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Call out your ideal customer explicitly, and get bonus points for calling out who you're NOT looking for. Saying no to customers polarizes the right ones toward you. When you advertise, you always advertise to one person. Specificity creates compelling copy by describing moments they experience, not vague labels.

- Call out your ideal customer; bonus for calling out who it's NOT for
- Saying no to customers polarizes right customers toward you
- Vanilla messaging ('everything to all people') means no one feels pulled
- Always advertise to one person - they self-select thinking 'this person is talking to me'
- Describe moments, not labels (not 'overweight' but the specific experience of being overweight)
- Five levels of awareness: unaware, problem aware, solution aware, product aware, most aware
- Specificity is what creates compelling copy

> "Instead of 'Are you overweight?' say 'Do you have trouble getting upstairs or going on a walk for a long period of time without a rash forming between your legs?'"
> "When you say no to customers, you polarize the right customers to move even more towards you."

## Clear Call to Action with Said-Do Correspondence (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Tell people exactly what to do next, what will happen after they do it, then ensure what you said happens actually happens. This builds 'said-do correspondence' - each fulfilled micro-promise increases your influence for the next instruction. Martha Stewart gained influence by giving recipes that produced exactly the promised result.

- Tell them exactly what to do next - they won't guess
- Tell them what will happen after they take action
- When what you said happens actually happens, you build 'said-do correspondence'
- Each fulfilled micro-promise increases influence for the next instruction
- Make promises, keep promises repeatedly = compounding influence
- They must take action and your promise must be fulfilled by their action
- Alex named company 'Acquisition.com' so the name IS the call to action

> "Martha Stewart was such a powerful influencer because she literally gave people recipes to follow."

## Cold Door Knocking with Social Proof Leverage (Source: Alex Hormozi)

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**

## Damaging Admission Formula (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Lead with an honest negative admission about your product, business, or self, then pivot to a compelling positive. The admission builds credibility, making the positive claim more believable. Claiming your own flaws preemptively removes them as weapons competitors or skeptics can use against you.

- Formula: Bad admission → BUT → amplified good
- Telling truth about a negative upfront makes positive claims more credible
- Small business negatives (small size, capacity constraints, local) are often genuine positives to the right audience
- If people will find out about your flaws anyway, control the narrative by claiming them first
- When you claim negatives, others cannot weaponize them against you
- Framing: the bad truth amplifies the good truth when sequenced correctly
- Eminem 8 Mile example: claimed all negative things others could say, leaving no ammunition for opponents

> "Tell the truth that isn't exciting upfront, then deliver good news. People will believe the good news more because you established credibility."
> "If you don't claim the flaw, they think it and doubt you. If you claim it, they can't hold it against you."

## Epiphany Bridge Testimonial Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A six-quadrant interview structure (Internal/External × Before/During/After) that maps a real customer's decision journey. Captures fears, objections, and the reasoning that overcame them, producing both persuasive content and ad creative.

- Six-quadrant matrix: Internal/External × Before/During/After
- Before Internal: emotional/psychological state (e.g., 'My wife didn't respect me because I couldn't make money')
- Before External: observable facts (e.g., $33K/month income, behind on payments, almost lost house)
- Decision Point External: trigger event (saw ad, testimonials from similar businesses)
- Decision Point Internal: initial objection (wasn't sure if it was legit)
- Why they moved forward: saw social proof from similar profiles
- After Internal: emotional outcome (restored dignity, better about myself)
- After External: observable results (got car back, upgraded neighborhood, kids in better school)
- *(+2 additional points)*

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- One interview generates three distinct marketing assets
- 60-90 second ad with highlight points

> "This framework is valuable because it shows the prospect's own internal dialogue—their fears, concerns, and the reasoning that overcame those fears. This is more persuasive than the founder explaining why someone should buy."
> "Seeing people similar to oneself winning is the strongest motivator for taking action."

## Five Levels of Customer Awareness (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Different levels of customer awareness require different advertising approaches: (1) Completely unaware - hook with curiosity, (2) Problem aware - describe their problem specifically, (3) Solution aware - reference solutions they've tried that failed, (4) Product aware - compare specific product variants, (5) Most aware - make direct offers. Specificity increases as awareness increases.

- Completely unaware: hook with pure curiosity
- Problem aware: 'Do you get up at night to pee more than two times?'
- Solution aware: 'Have you tried this software and it didn't work?'
- Product aware: 'Have you tried this type of creatine versus this type?'
- Most aware: make offers to these people directly
- Match your copy approach to the audience's awareness level

## Four-Angle Persuasion Framework (Meat Copy) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A four-quadrant persuasion structure covering all emotional angles of a buying decision: positive reinforcement (more good), negative relief (less bad), negative consequence of inaction (more bad), and opportunity cost of inaction (less good). Applied to ad body copy to maximize motivation to act.

- Angle 1: You will get more good stuff if you do what I want
- Angle 2: You will get less bad stuff if you do what I want
- Angle 3: You will get more bad stuff if you don't do what I want
- Angle 4: You will get less good stuff if you don't do what I want
- Example: Gym membership — feel better (more good), no brain fog (less bad), brain fog worsens in 2-3 years without action (more bad), fewer happy memories with family (less good)

> "You will get more good stuff if you do what I want. You will get less bad stuff if you do what I want. You will get more bad stuff if you don't do what I want. You will get less good stuff if you don't do what I want."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Angle 3: You will get more bad stuff if you don't do what I want
- Angle 4: You will get less good stuff if you don't do what I want

## Gary Halbert's Hypothetical Maximum (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Start by imagining the absolute most effective, highest-touch version of your outreach as if your life depended on a single attempt. Execute that unscalable version, then systematically identify which 20% of elements drove 80% of results, and scale those elements while cutting the rest.

- Imagine you can only send one letter or you die - what would you do?
- Start with handwritten envelope, handwritten name, licked stamp, handwritten letter, lumpy mail
- If you only have budget for 100 letters, handwrite all of them
- Use revenue from the first 100 to scale to 500
- Identify which 20% of the unscalable elements drove 80% of the results
- Use cash from earlier less-scalable operations to create leverage in more-scalable operations
- You always lose efficiency at scale, so start with the absolute most efficient process

> "You can use cash from your earlier less scalable thing to create more leverage in the more scalable thing and get 80% of the effect with 10 times the output."
> "Whenever I want to write a sales letter, I imagine I can only write one letter for my life or I'm going to die."

## Give in Public, Sell in Private (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A monetization framework for content creators: maximize public value delivery to build goodwill and audience, then convert through private conversations (DMs, replies, calls) rather than aggressive public CTAs. Light-touch public CTAs are acceptable but heavy selling should happen one-on-one.

- Public content should be pure value — no aggressive selling — to build goodwill and grow audience.
- DMs and story replies are private contexts where interest is already signaled; selling there is appropriate.
- Acceptable public CTA: 'If you need help with that, I'm happy to help' in comments or stories.
- Transition line in private: 'I've given you all this value. If you want more, I'd be happy to hop on a call.'
- This approach allows monetization without damaging the goodwill and trust built through content.
- Public content = 100% value, zero selling
- Monetization occurs only in private channels (DMs, email, etc.)
- Leverages reciprocity: people who receive value feel indebted and are more likely to comply with later requests
- *(+3 additional points)*

> "Give in public, sell in private. When people respond to your stories or DM you, those are conversations where they're usually asking or signaling interest."
> "When you give, you deposit goodwill into the audience. If you give someone something first, they're far more likely to comply with your request later."

## Six Categories of Sales Objections (Source: Alex Hormozi)

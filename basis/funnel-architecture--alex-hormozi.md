## 100% Commission Affiliate Architecture (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Instead of offering standard 20% affiliate commissions (which don't motivate partners), peel off one component of your product bundle and give 100% of its revenue to the affiliate. This creates an irresistible incentive for affiliates while delivering pre-sold, qualified leads at low CAC.

- Standard 20% affiliate commissions don't motivate partners—they built trust and relationships to hand customers over for 20%
- Peel off one product component and give 100% of its revenue to the affiliate
- Affiliate keeps all revenue from component; you acquire a pre-sold, high-quality customer for cost-of-delivery only
- Psychology is completely different: not splitting revenue but giving them their own revenue stream
- Affiliates use it as an upsell or bundle into existing products
- Works best when aggregating many small businesses rather than chasing mega-influencers
- Discounts below 50% don't change behavior; 100% commission on a component changes behavior dramatically
- The lead shows up pre-paid and highly qualified—no ad spend, no landing pages, no follow-up needed

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Massage business example: affiliate sells 3 massages at $500, keeps $500; your cost is $90 (3 × $30 labor)
- If 1 in 3 of those leads convert to $2,000 product: CAC = $270, customer value = $2,000
- 10% discount coupons don't move behavior; 50%+ discounts generate real demand changes

> "From your perspective: If your cost per massage is $30 (labor), you pay $90 to acquire a customer who spent $500—a highly qualified lead."
> "Make it so good that it hurts. Give the affiliate enough upside that they'll push hard."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Standard 20% affiliate commissions don't motivate partners—they built trust and relationships to hand customers over for 20%
- Discounts below 50% don't change behavior; 100% commission on a component changes behavior dramatically

## 30-Day Payback Period Rule (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Measure what a customer is worth within the first 30 days, not over their entire lifetime. This is the practical limit for most small businesses from a cash flow perspective and aligns with the interest-free period on credit cards. Managing the payback period of when you get money back determines how quickly you can grow and reinvest.

- 30-day payback enables use of credit card float (interest-free money) to fund growth with zero net cash outlay
- Mechanism: spend on credit card → acquire customer → customer pays before card bill due → repeat infinitely
- Mechanisms to pull cash forward: initiation fees, setup fees, onboarding programs, prepay discounts, extended guarantee bundles, buy-one-get-two, upfront last-month payment
- Funded/large businesses with capital reserves can tolerate longer payback periods
- Bootstrapped businesses must prioritize sub-30-day payback for limitless growth
- Every business that scaled significantly for the speaker recovered CAC within 30 days
- 30 days is the maximum most small businesses can wait to recover customer acquisition costs
- 30 days matches the interest-free period on credit cards
- *(+3 additional points)*

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Credit cards provide ~30 days interest-free float
- Gym Launch and all other scaled businesses recovered CAC within 30 days
- Alex's gym pre-sale model: $5K initial -> $100K in 30 days -> fully financed gym opening

> "Take $100 of credit card money. Use it to get a customer. By month-end, make that $100 back. You owe no one anything and now have a customer. Repeat infinitely."
> "The number one rule of business is don't go out of business. And so what's the thing that prevents you from going out of business? Cash flow."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Bootstrapped businesses must prioritize sub-30-day payback for limitless growth
- Number one rule of business: don't go out of business, which means managing cash flow

## 60-Second Lead Response Rule (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Responding to opt-in leads within 60 seconds multiplies sales by 4x. Delayed response allows competitor comparison, triggers pricing wars, lowers close rates, and increases effective CAC.

- Calling leads within 60 seconds of opt-in 4x sales vs. delayed response
- Delayed response allows leads to gather competitor information, initiating price comparison
- Price wars reduce gross margins and increase effective CAC simultaneously
- Objection 'I can't afford to hire someone to call leads that fast' is self-defeating: 4x revenue enables hiring
- Lead response time is an indicator of operational excellence dedication
- Faster response = higher close rates = lower effective CAC = better LTV:CAC ratio

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Studies show calling leads quickly 4x's sales
- Delayed response can increase effective CAC by up to 4x

> "Lead response time reveals how dedicated to excellence you are."
> "Studies prove that calling leads quickly 4xs your sales."

## Affiliate Low-Friction Onboarding Principle (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Affiliate signup forms should minimize friction to maximize volume. Four fields (first name, last name, email, phone) and 30-second completion time. Deliver the tracking link immediately on thank-you page and in confirmation email.

- More friction = higher quality affiliates but lower volume
- Less friction = more volume, appropriate for mass-market products like books
- Provide custom tracking URL on both thank-you page and confirmation email
- Changing the banner above the affiliate signup form increased signups by 60%
- Dual delivery of tracking link (thank-you page + email) creates a meaningful difference in activation

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Changing banner above form increased affiliate signups by 60%
- Providing tracking link on thank-you page plus email yields 25% difference in activation
- 10,000 people signed up as affiliates for the book launch campaign

> "The affiliate opt-in form requires: first name, last name, email, phone. That's it. 30 seconds to sign up."
> "More friction = higher quality. Less friction = more volume."

## B2B Affiliate Activation System (Launch + Integrate Model) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A three-phase system for maximizing revenue from existing B2B referral partners: Trial (send first patient/client), Launch (campaign to drive volume with incentives), and Integrate (embed referral process into partner's workflow). Most businesses stall at Trial and never complete Launch or Integrate.

- Phase 1 - Trial: Send 1 patient/referral to measure fit and establish relationship
- Phase 2 - Launch: Run dedicated campaign to physician/partner—'send us 10 patients this month'
- Phase 3 - Integrate: Embed referral process into partner's workflow (EHR integration, standardized referral forms, automated follow-ups)
- Hire dedicated affiliate manager (account rep) to own weekly touchpoints with top partners
- Weekly in-person visits with top affiliates (bring donuts, coffee, physical presence)
- After each patient treatment, contact referring doctor with results and ask: 'which other patients from this week would benefit?'
- Reactivation campaigns: remind physicians they exist and prompt continued referrals
- Patient experience must be exceptional to incentivize physician continued advocacy

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 200+ physician/therapist affiliates enrolled
- Cost to acquire affiliate: ~$500
- Average revenue per affiliate: $6,000/year (1 patient per doctor referral)
- B2B ROI: 12:1
- Potential per physician if activated: $30,000-50,000/year (5-10 referrals/month)

> "After treatment, contact referring doctor with results, ask 'which other patients from this week would benefit?'"
> "Only getting 1 patient per doctor, not 20-50 potential"

## B2B Before B2C Sequencing Principle (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A capital allocation principle stating that businesses with a working B2B affiliate channel should maximize that channel's output before investing in B2C advertising, because B2B has proven ROI, built-in advocacy, exponential scaling through networks, and lower incremental cost than B2C paid acquisition.

- Maximize B2B affiliate activation before starting B2C paid advertising
- B2B has built-in advocacy (doctors continue referring for years vs. one-time consumer purchase)
- B2B scales exponentially through affiliate networks; consumer ads scale linearly with spend
- Cost of entry comparable when B2B is underactivated: both acquire at same transaction value
- Only pursue B2C after B2B is fully squeezed in multiple metro markets
- Replicate B2B playbook in second market before adding B2C complexity

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- B2B ROI proven at 12:1 ($500 acquisition cost, $6,000/year revenue)
- B2B physician capacity utilization: ~1% of potential (1 referral/year vs. 50+ possible)

> "B2B has built-in advocacy (doctors continue referring for years vs. one-time consumer purchase)"
> "Maximize B2B before starting B2C"

## BAMF - Book A Meeting From A Meeting (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Never let a prospect or customer fall into no-man's land. Always book the next meeting before ending the current one. In enterprise sales, reframe the timeline: 'This doesn't take 12 months, it takes 48 hours of work — we can stretch it over 2 years or do it in 3 months.' Always move the ball forward.

- BAMF: Book A Meeting From A Meeting — never end without the next meeting scheduled
- If you can't push the ball forward from one meeting to the next, it's a lost sale
- Reframe enterprise timelines: 'It takes 48 hours of work, not 12 months of time'
- Let the prospect decide if they want to compress or stretch the timeline

> "BAMF — book a meeting from a meeting. We never have anyone falling through the cracks. We always know what we're doing to move the ball forward."

## BAND Qualification Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A four-criteria qualification gate applied before any sales call: Budget (has money), Authority (can make decision alone), Need (actually needs the product), Timing (now is the right time). Only prospects meeting all four criteria receive sales calls.

- Budget: Does the prospect have money to spend?
- Authority: Can they make the purchasing decision themselves without needing approval?
- Need: Do they actually need this product/service?
- Timing: Is now the right time for them to buy?
- Prospects must answer yes to ALL four to receive a sales call
- Fully qualified prospects who have consumed value are vastly more likely to close

> "A call with a fully qualified prospect who has consumed value, understands the offering, has money and authority, and has the need and timing is vastly more likely to close."
> "Only take calls from people who answer yes to all four."

## BANT Qualification Framework for Lead Scoring (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Qualify leads using BANT: Budget, Authority, Need, and Timing. Good friction is friction that filters out unqualified leads while keeping good ones. Bad friction (slow page loads, duplicate forms) removes everyone. Ask the fewest questions required to make the qualify/disqualify decision. If unqualified, still give them a free thing so they feel valued.

- Budget: Can they afford the core offer price ($5,800)?
- Authority: Can they make the decision alone without others?
- Need: Do they need this solution right now?
- Timing: Are they willing to move forward today?
- Prioritize leads who answer 'Today' or 'Tomorrow' on timing dropdown
- Qualification friction self-selects high-intent prospects
- BANT: Budget, Authority (can they decide?), Need (how bad?), Timing (when do they want to start?)
- Good friction: increases lead quality, removes bad leads only
- *(+4 additional points)*

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Core offer price used as budget qualifier: $5,800
- Timing dropdown: Today / Tomorrow / Few weeks — only close Today/Tomorrow respondents

> "'Can you afford $5,800 for IP protection?' / 'Is anyone else involved in this decision?' / 'What happens if you don't solve this?' / 'When would you like to start?'"
> "Good friction is something that increases the overall quality of the lead and gets out people who suck. Bad friction gets everyone out."

## Before-During-After Service Conversion Process (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Structured three-phase customer interaction process that captures data, sells memberships, and secures future appointments. Each phase has a specific conversion objective tied to the customer's natural state of engagement.

- Before service: ID confirmation, payment card on file, sign-in form with referral source dropdown, review collection, ad attribution tracking
- During service: Membership offer presented while customer is captive for 60 minutes getting lashes done
- After service: Prepayment incentive framed as 'making today free' through accumulated discounts
- Sequence exploits natural engagement windows rather than interrupting service flow
- First discount applied immediately at point of sale to create tangible proof of membership value

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Customers are seated and captive for approximately 60 minutes during lash application
- Initial membership test: 5 existing clients approached, 2 converted (40% close rate)
- Upsell from $99 intro to $170+ premium achieved 90% of the time by Annie but only 50% by staff

> "Before-service process: Get ID confirmation, confirm payment card on file, customer sign-in, track referral source and ad attribution, collect reviews. During service: Membership offer made before lashes. After service: Offer prepayment incentive. Frame year-long prepay as 'making today free' through accumulated discounts."

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- If 100 students complete entry level, maybe 20 pursue next level, maybe 2 pursue highest level

## Content-to-Business Throughput Model (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Wide content (relationships, fitness, general advice) generates more overall views but dramatically lower throughput to high-quality business applications and book sales. Making exclusively business and business-adjacent content produces fewer total views but much higher quality leads that actually convert to revenue.

- Tested 90 days of wide content vs business-focused content
- Wide content got more views but throughput to applications and book sales went way down
- Shorts viewers do not become Longs viewers — consumption preferences are platform-specific
- People who watch Longs want more Longs; people who watch Shorts want more Shorts
- Business-adjacent content (personal development, productivity, time management) is acceptable; relationship content is too many steps removed

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 90-day test of wide content strategy
- 8 million followers across platforms over 3 years
- 35,000 pieces of content produced

> "We got more overall views but in terms of throughput of from those views to high quality applications into the business or just even book sales went way down"
> "The idea that like I do a relationship video and then business owners also have relationships, it's too many steps to get the right people"

## Custom vs More CTA Strategy (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Instead of offering 'more' content as a CTA (which is overused since infinite content exists by scrolling), offer 'custom' — personalized deliverables based on individual circumstances. Custom requires going through your funnel, while 'more' is available everywhere for free.

- More content is available infinitely by scrolling — it is not a compelling CTA
- Custom content (e.g., custom recipes based on your macros) requires going through your system
- Scale custom through group calls and small groups
- Everyone says 'you can get more' — differentiate by offering customization

> "Instead of offering more, offer custom. If you're on organic there's an infinite amount of more content, they just keep scrolling. But to get custom they have to go through your thing."

## Date Night Challenge Funnel (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Instead of spreading content across a 5-day challenge (which loses half the audience between day 1 and 2), compress it into a single 2-4 hour event framed as a 'date night.' This doubles attendance for the pitch, maintains audience attention in flow state, and eliminates intro/outro overhead between days.

- 5-day challenges lose ~50% of audience between day 1 and day 2
- A single extended session keeps people in state/flow and top of mind
- The date night frame gives natural permission for an extended event
- Super long single-day or half-day events are proven effective for selling high-ticket
- 3-4 hours of quality content with a wrap-up pitch at the end

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 9% show rate to day 1, dropping to 4-5% by day 2
- 49% of attendees book a sales call -- the issue was attendance, not conversion
- 511 leads per challenge, 26 average attendees, 12 calls booked, 6 sales closed

> "There's a certain amount of information that someone requires to make a decision. We can spread that in 5 hours over 5 days or we can do one 5 hour session. If I had to pick between the two, I'd rather do the 5 hour session."

## Dual Business Model Decision Framework (B2B Recurring vs. Media/Consumer) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Every business faces a fork between B2B recurring revenue (stable, valuable for exit, slow to build) and media/consumer viral growth (fast-scaling, more delegable, requires constant content innovation). Attempting both simultaneously creates 'no man's land' where neither is served effectively.

- Model A (B2B Recurring): High LTV, 12-year average customer lifetime, outbound sales required, valuable for acquisition
- Model B (Media/Consumer): Problem to solve is always finding cheap media channels, requires trend tracking, faster 10x growth potential
- Cannot pursue both simultaneously — 'no man's land' strategy serves neither master
- Choose based on: speed preference, delegation preference, skill set, exit goal
- B2B path: Slower growth, harder execution, better acquisition multiple
- Media path: Faster growth, more delegable, platform-dependency risk

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- B2B recurring: 12-year average customer lifetime
- Media path: 10x revenue potential vs. 2-3x for B2B path
- December TikTok viral event: $250,000 revenue in one month from 20M+ view video

> "Gag gift business: Extremely shareable, meme-optimized, low price sensitivity. Recurring trophy business (B2B): High price sensitivity, relationship-driven, slower CAC."
> "Cannot pursue both effectively; 'no man's land' strategy serves neither master."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Choose based on: speed preference, delegation preference, skill set, exit goal

## Event Lockout and Anticipation Strategy (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Move events behind a locked paywall after 6-12 months of membership to shift psychological framing from FOMO to anticipation. Announce the next event at the conclusion of the current event to sustain desire through the gap.

- Lock events behind paywall after 6-12 months of membership
- Shifts psychology from 'I'm missing out' to 'I can't wait until I can access this'
- Announce next event at the conclusion of the current event
- Upsells happen at the point of greatest need, not greatest satisfaction
- Pitching after a full event ('steak dinner' moment) fails because pain is temporarily relieved
- Announcement at event end sustains desire through gap between events
- Increases perceived scarcity and ongoing engagement
- $99 two-day virtual event can be repurposed as a high-value bonus

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Two monthly in-person events increased churn rate at Gym Launch
- 6-12 month paywall lock timeline

> "Move events behind a locked paywall after 6-12 months of membership. This shifts psychology from 'I'm missing out' to 'I can't wait until I can access this.'"
> "Announce the upcoming event while they're still in a state of desire."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Pitching after a full event ('steak dinner' moment) fails because pain is temporarily relieved

## Five Distribution Levers Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

There are only five fundamental levers for growing any business: one-on-one outreach, content creation, paid ads, affiliates/referrals, and agencies/employees. Understanding this constraint forces entrepreneurs to pick and execute one lever rather than being paralyzed by unlimited perceived options.

- Lever 1: Talk to people one-on-one (direct outreach).
- Lever 2: Make content (organic distribution).
- Lever 3: Run ads (paid distribution).
- Lever 4: Get affiliates or referrals (partner distribution).
- Lever 5: Hire agencies or employees (delegated distribution).
- Recognizing the limited number of levers removes decision paralysis and forces execution.

> "You have limited levers: talk to people one-on-one, make content, run ads, get affiliates or referrals, hire agencies or employees. Those are your options."

- Tier 3 (add-on): Split margin between customer discount and affiliate commission from existing margin pool
- True CAC calculation: if 1 of 2 affiliate-introduced customers upsells, CAC = cost of first customer's product delivery
- High upsell multiplier: customer buys one unit but needs 10 more (compounding referral value)
- Raise direct consumer prices to fund affiliate margin without eroding company economics
- Never count the other guy's money—focus on your revenue, not affiliate's profit

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Standard kickback: 20% commission (baseline, ineffective)
- Preferred model: 100% markup retained by affiliate
- Example CAC math: $0.50 product cost; 1-of-2 upsells = $0.50 effective CAC

## Two-Path Sales Call Close System (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Structure every sales call with exactly two explicit outcomes disclosed in advance: close at the primary high-ticket price, or downsell to the education product. Prevents wasted calls with no outcome and captures value from unqualified prospects.

- Path 1: Qualified lead → Sell services at $5,800 + 5% commission
- Path 2: Unqualified lead → Offer education downsell at $2,000-$3,000
- Both paths disclosed to prospect before call via VSL
- Education downsell priced higher ($2K-$3K) than standalone offering ($500+$500) because phone qualification adds value signal
- No call should end without an offer made to the prospect
- Team capacity: 3+ salespeople each handling 3-4 calls/day

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Primary close price: $5,800
- Downsell price: $2,000-$3,000
- Current education standalone price: $500 + $500 upsell
- Team capacity: 3 people × 3-4 calls/day

> "There are two possible outcomes on every call — they buy services at $5,800 or they get offered the education program as a downgrade."
> "The education product is priced higher on the phone because the qualification process itself adds perceived value."

## VSSL Pre-Sale System (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Place a video sales letter (VSSL) before the human sales conversation. The first VSSL on the thank-you page covers pain and promise. A second VSSL before the scheduled call handles top objections and call prep. This frontloads the repetitive pitch so the live call starts from a place of making a decision rather than educating from scratch.

- Take everything you repeat in every sales call and frontload it into video
- VSSL allows charts, graphs, statistics, and visuals that phone calls can't provide
- Perfect pitch delivered every time in automated format
- Customization becomes what the live conversation is about
- Set call team should tell prospects to watch the video: 'First question he'll ask is did you watch it'
- Post-booking page should have VSSL + lead magnet download + call prep

> "I would rather have the perfect pitch delivered every time in an automated format and then have the customization be what the conversation is after the fact."

## Water and Food Deprivation Selling Model (Source: Alex Hormozi)

When someone is thirsty, sell them water. But immediately after giving them water, they are no longer thirsty — now they are hungry. Sell food at the point of greatest deprivation for food. The free lead magnet satisfies one need (water) and creates deprivation for the paid core offer (food), which should be significantly more expensive.

- Free offer satisfies one need and creates deprivation for the next
- Do not solve the core problem with the free offer
- Solve a problem that leads to the core issue
- Make the paid offer significantly more expensive than the free one
- When someone pays with time now, they are more likely to pay with money later

> "Don't solve the problem your core product solves with the free offer. But do solve a problem that leads to your core issue."
> "When someone pays with time now, they're more likely to pay with money later"

## 1,000 True Fans Revenue Model (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A small number of highly engaged, niche-specific fans who deeply believe in your expertise is sufficient to build a six-to-seven figure business. True fans purchase repeatedly and at higher price points because of extreme relevance and trust, making audience size irrelevant compared to audience depth.

- 1,000 true fans at $10/month = $100,000/year baseline
- True fans buy at high-ticket price points ($4,000-$5,000) because the ROI is clear and specific to their problem
- People who like you a lot (deep niche resonance) buy; people who like you a little (broad content) do not
- Audience depth (engagement, trust, relevance) drives revenue; audience breadth drives fame
- You can either optimize for rich or famous — niche optimizes for rich, broad optimizes for famous

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 1,000 fans x $10/month = $100,000/year
- RD specialist: 2,900 email list, ~$1M/year profit
- First sale made with 8 email subscribers

> "You only need a thousand true fans. A thousand fans. You can do whatever you want with a thousand fans even at 10 bucks a month."
> "The people who like you a lot are the ones who buy from you."

## 1,000-Hour Product Depth Rule (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Truly excellent products require 1,000+ hours of focused work — far more than typical creators invest. The depth of craft only emerges from repeated exposure to the product across many emotional and temporal states, revealing details invisible at lower investment levels.

- Most people spend ~100 hours or fewer on a product; 1,000 hours produces genuinely good work
- Start a clock when working, stop when not working — accumulate raw hours on one thing
- Reviewing a product when cold, hot, angry, sad, inspired, and bored reveals different flaws and opportunities
- Quality gap between levels is exponential: 8→9 is 10x work; 9→9.5 is another 10x; 9.5→9.75 is another 10x
- The ability to sustain unbroken focus for long enough to accumulate these hours is itself a trainable skill

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Quality improvement from 8 to 9 requires 10x the effort of reaching an 8
- Quality improvement from 9 to 9.5 requires another 10x effort
- Quality improvement from 9.5 to 9.75 requires another 10x effort

> "I can promise you if you put 1,000 hours into something it'll probably be pretty good. Most people spend like 100 hours on something. That's nothing."
> "You see it with different lenses. And then what happens is it gives you so much more depth and context to whatever you're trying to build."

**Common Pitfalls:**

## 10x Price Frame (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Three frames for creating premium offers: (1) What would you include if you charged 10x-100x more? (2) If growth depended only on word-of-mouth from one customer, what would you build? (3) Remove everything unscalable but make it worth 10x as much.

- Frame 1: What if we charged 10x or 100x more — write down everything, cross out hard costs, sell what remains
- Frame 2: If only word-of-mouth could grow it, what would the customer experience look like?
- Frame 3: Remove all unscalable elements but make it worth 10x as much
- Confronting the high price is essential — allow the prospect full consideration
- After stating the premium price, shut up and let them talk
- If they balk, offer 90% of elements in a scalable format

> "You must confront the high price. You have to confront it."
> "An amount greater than zero will say yes."

## 13 Competitive Moats Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Thirteen strategies for building monopoly-like competitive advantages in business. Each moat creates barriers to entry that make it progressively harder for competitors to challenge your position. The concept draws from the Greek root of monopoly: mono (one) + poles (to sell).

- Network Effects: Value increases as more people join (customer base or product ecosystem)
- Exclusive Control Over Resources or Technology: Own supply or data that competitors cannot replicate
- Government Regulations and Licensing: Secure exclusive grants or licenses to operate
- Economies of Scale: Achieve lower cost per unit through larger volume, undercutting competitors
- Vertical Integration: Control supply chain from sourcing to retail, capturing margin at every step
- Strong Brand Identity: Build associations so brand becomes synonymous with the product category
- Strategic Pricing: Use predatory pricing or price discrimination to block competitors
- Exclusive Contracts: Lock up supply, customers, or distribution through long-term contracts
- *(+5 additional points)*

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Con Edison revenue approximately $16 billion last year (over $1 billion per month)
- Allen software scaled from 100 to 4,000-5,000 appointments daily
- Amazon sellers pay 50-67% of revenue to Amazon
- Monster Entertainment paid $500 million to buy rights to its own recipe
- 5,000+ gyms in gym launch network

> "Own 20% of a billion-dollar company rather than 100% of a million-dollar company"
> "Small businesses stay small through competition. Large businesses consolidate."

## 30-Day Cash Collected = COGs + CAC Rule (Source: Alex Hormozi)

The specific cash collection target for the first 30 days of a customer relationship must exceed COGs (cost of goods/services) plus CAC. Achieving this makes customer acquisition self-funding and enables compound growth.

- Formula: 30-day cash collected > COGs + CAC
- When this condition is met, each customer acquisition is fully self-funded; proceed to acquire next customer
- Complements the 30-day payback period rule with a specific mathematical target
- Manufacturing businesses have different COGs structures but 80%+ gross margins still preferred
- Human services businesses: always target 80%+ gross margins

> "Customer comes in, you acquire them, deliver on them, they pay you back all that cost. Then what? Go get another customer."
> "What exact amount of money do you want collected within 30 days? It's COG plus CAC."

## 80% Gross Margin Architecture for Service Businesses (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Service businesses must maintain minimum 80% gross margin to fund marketing, operations, sales, and target 20-30% net profit. Calculate maximum allowable delivery hours per engagement by dividing 20% of price by hourly labor cost.

- Gross margin floor: 80% minimum
- Cost of delivery ceiling: 20% of price
- At $5,800 price: maximum $1,160 in delivery costs
- At $100/hour lawyer cost: maximum 11.6 hours of labor per engagement
- Remaining 80% gross margin allocated: 30-40% marketing, 20-30% admin/ops, portion for sales, remainder is net profit
- Net profit target: 20-30% of revenue

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Price point: $5,800
- 20% cost ceiling: $1,160
- At $100/hour labor: 11 maximum hours per engagement
- Marketing allocation: 30-40% of revenue
- Admin/ops allocation: 20-30% of revenue

> "If your lawyer costs $100 an hour, you can only afford 11 hours of work per engagement and still hit your margin."
> "You need a minimum 80% gross margin — cost of delivery must be less than $1,100 on a $5,800 engagement."

## Arbitrage as Core Business Principle (Source: Alex Hormozi)

All profit comes from some form of arbitrage - the difference between what something costs you and what you sell it for. This can be geographic (living cheap, selling expensive), network-based (paying for access to wealthy circles), or skill-based (knowing how to do something for less than what others would pay).

- Wealth is a ratio of income to expenses, not an absolute number
- Geographic arbitrage: live where it is cheap, sell to expensive markets
- Network arbitrage: overpay for access to people who can change your trajectory
- Skill arbitrage: the difference between what it costs you to deliver vs what you charge
- The zip code tax means people charge more based on perceived wealth

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Thailand median income $2,700/year vs US median $50-60K - more than 10x arbitrage
- Saved $50K while living on $400/month rent sharing a spare bedroom

> "Wealth is a ratio. It's not a number. It's how much you spend relative to how much you make"

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Skill arbitrage: the difference between what it costs you to deliver vs what you charge

## Attack Vectors Framework for Problem Solving (Source: Alex Hormozi)

The number of distinct approaches (attack vectors) a person deploys against a problem is a direct proxy for their standards. High-standard operators iterate through dozens of angles toward the same goal rather than declaring failure after one or two attempts.

- Measure someone's standards by counting how many different approaches they use to solve one problem
- It's not about repeating the same action 100 times — it's 100 different iterations toward the same goal
- Declaring 'it didn't work' after 1-2 attempts means you weren't skilled enough, not that the approach is invalid
- Every problem you have has been solved by someone else — the question is what cost you're unwilling to bear
- Each attack vector has a cost; evaluate whether the cost is worth the result, not whether the vector is comfortable

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Hiring 15 SDRs in 1 month vs. 3 months = $4M additional Q1 profit
- Example attack vectors for hiring: $100K referral bounty, acquiring a company with existing SDR team, hiring 5 recruiters, flying reps in for 3-day vs. 30-day onboarding, using senior reps as onboarding leads

> "It's not about doing the same thing 100 times. That's not smart. It's about trying to accomplish the same goal with 100 different iterations and angles until one finally gets through."
> "It's not that it didn't work. It's that you weren't skilled enough to make it work. Very big difference."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Declaring 'it didn't work' after 1-2 attempts means you weren't skilled enough, not that the approach is invalid

## Barbell Customer Model (Source: Alex Hormozi)

- The middle is where businesses die: mid-price offerings to small customers creates all six wrong-customer red flags simultaneously

> "The middle is where people die: mid-price offerings to small customers creates short lifespans, excessive support demands, price resistance, dissatisfaction despite effort, and overpromising."

## Close Rate to Pricing Multiplier Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A rule-of-thumb mapping between close rates and degree of underpricing. High close rates signal significant underpricing; below 30% signals avatar or sales motion problems, not price problems. Raising price is almost always the correct direction for service businesses.

- 80%+ close rate = underpriced 3-4x
- 60-80% close rate = underpriced 2-3x
- 50-60% close rate = underpriced 1.5-2x
- 40-50% close rate = underpriced 1.25-1.5x
- 30-40% close rate = appropriately priced, assuming full educational sales motion in place
- Below 30% close rate = avatar mismatch or broken sales motion; fix before touching price
- For SaaS/unlimited scale, price balances growth rate vs. gross margin; lower price increases growth, higher price increases margin
- For 80% of service businesses with finite capacity, price direction is always up
- *(+1 additional points)*

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 80% of businesses are service-based and unscalable, meaning raising price is the only direction
- 5% of businesses are SaaS where price-growth tradeoff matters

> "Below 30%, you either have an avatar issue, a sales motion issue, or both. Fix those two first before ever considering lowering price."
> "If you're closing at 80% or more, you're typically underpriced by 3 to 4x."

## Column A / Column B Value Separation (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Every educational offering contains two distinct value categories: Column A (one-time value — knowledge or skills that once learned no longer require ongoing teaching) and Column B (consumables — things people must use repeatedly). These must be priced separately or customers exit once one-time value is extracted.

- One-time value: price as high as market will bear
- Consumables: price based on standalone value of the consumable, not as a payment plan spread
- Customers exit when they've extracted the one-time value if the consumable isn't worth the full monthly price
- Teaching someone to sell is one-time value; once they know, they graduate

> "People exit when they've extracted the one-time value because the consumable isn't worth the full monthly price."
> "The critical mistake: pricing consumables as a spread-out version of one-time value."

## Compounding Business Model via Renewal Architecture (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Annual billing with low churn creates a compounding revenue base where prior-year customers renew alongside new customer acquisition, growing revenue without proportional acquisition cost increases. Acquisition-dependent businesses die when channels fail; renewal-dependent businesses survive.

- Year 1: 100 customers × $5,000 annual = $500,000
- Year 2: 80 renewals ($400,000) + 100 new ($500,000) = $900,000
- Year 3: 64 from year 1 + 80 from year 2 + 100 new = $1,220,000
- Monthly billing at 10.7% churn requires replacing 90% of customers monthly — unsustainable acquisition burden
- Acquisition-dependent businesses fail immediately when channel shuts down (Facebook, YouTube, email domains)
- Renewal-dependent businesses survive channel loss because existing customers continue paying

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Year 1: $500,000 — Year 2: $900,000 — Year 3: $1,220,000 at 2% annual churn, 100 new customers/year, $5,000 price

> "Business vulnerability: Acquisition-dependent businesses die immediately when channel shuts down. Renewal-dependent businesses survive channel loss because existing customers continue paying."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Acquisition-dependent businesses fail immediately when channel shuts down (Facebook, YouTube, email domains)

## Difficulty as Competitive Moat (Source: Alex Hormozi)

The difficulty of entering or executing in a business is itself a competitive barrier. High difficulty means fewer competitors, which is an advantage, not a deterrent. The size of an obstacle is proportional to the value of the goal.

- More difficult business entry = fewer entrances = less competition
- Barriers to entry are features, not bugs
- The level of difficulty to achieve a goal is the same barrier everyone else must clear — so difficulty selects for fewer competitors
- Big goals have big obstacles; that's what makes them worthwhile and rare
- Easy goals lose value because universal access eliminates scarcity

> "The level of difficulty you have to go through to get whatever you want is literally the barrier everyone else has to go through to achieve it too."
> "The more difficult it is to get into a business, the fewer entrances there will be and the less competition you have."

## Dividend Recapitalization Strategy (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A business owner uses the entire business as collateral to take a loan from a bank, receiving a large lump sum while the business carries the debt. The loan proceeds are tax-free, and if the business pays off the debt, the owner received the cash and still owns the business outright.

- Use entire business as collateral — no employees involved
- Bank loans a multiple of annual income (e.g., 5x earnings)
- Business carries the debt, not the owner personally
- Loan proceeds are tax-free to the owner
- Business pays off debt over time (analogous to paying off a mortgage)
- Owner retains full ownership of the business after debt is paid

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- $100M annual income business can yield ~$500M loan from bank
- Hypothetical payoff period: five years
- Loan proceeds are tax-free

> "Go directly to the bank and ask how much they'll loan you using your entire business as collateral. If you reliably produce $100 million in income, the bank might give you a $500 million loan."
> "The business carries the debt, not you personally. You get $500 million. If your business grows and pays off the debt in five years, you got the $500 million and still own the business."

## Done-for-You vs Do-It-Yourself Avatar Selection (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Whether to offer done-for-you or do-it-yourself depends on how much the customer makes. If they make $35K/year, the value of learning the skill exceeds their current skill level — DIY makes sense. If they make $1M/year, they can pay someone less than they make to do it — DFY makes sense. Match the service model to the avatar's income level.

- Low-income avatars benefit from learning skills themselves (DIY)
- High-income avatars benefit from paying others to do it (DFY)
- The breakpoint: can they pay someone less than they make per hour to do the task?

Within any broad market exist sub-segments that are severely underserved, have high purchasing power, and have no well-matched offer available. Positioning in front of this 'starving crowd' makes offer quality secondary to positioning.

- The best competitive advantage is positioning — being in front of hungry buyers at the right moment
- A starving crowd buys anything vaguely resembling a solution
- Look for sub-segments that are: wildly underserved, have money, and have no one presenting the right offer
- Positioning beats product quality when the crowd is sufficiently hungry

> "You can sell anything vaguely resembling a hot dog. You can sell shit inside a bun. A starving crowd will buy."

## The Tesla Model (DFY → DWY → DIY Pyramid) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Start at the top of the service pyramid with the most expensive done-for-you (DFY) offering, then work downward to done-with-you (DWY) at 1/3 to 1/5 the price, then do-it-yourself (DIY) at 1/5 to 1/10 of DWY. This approach maximizes early revenue, develops SOPs, creates price anchoring, and positions the brand from the top down.

- Start DFY: easy to sell, hard to fulfill, but few customers so manageable; maximizes revenue per customer
- DFY develops SOPs and productizes the service for later tiers
- Move to DWY at 1/3 to 1/5 of DFY price; sell 10X more people with 1/10th the support
- DIY takes all SOPs and gives them to customers at 1/5 to 1/10 of DWY price
- Top-down approach creates price anchoring and brand positioning at the premium end
- Operational drag is lowest at top (fewest customers) and increases going down
- Sales skill improves over time from spending time with customers, making downstream selling easier
- Named after Tesla's approach: started with Roadster (expensive), then Model S, then Model 3

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- DFY example: 10 private clients at $10,000/month
- DWY: 1/3 to 1/5 of DFY price
- DIY: 1/5 to 1/10 of DWY price

> "I have 10 private clients who each pay me $10,000 a month. A lot of you are asking, but you don't necessarily qualify for my private client service. So I took all the stuff I have for my private client service and made it available to you."
> "I recommend following what I call the Tesla Model. You start at the top of the pyramid with the most expensive thing first: done for you."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Sales skill improves over time from spending time with customers, making downstream selling easier

## The Three P's Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Almost all businesses are born from one of three P's: Pain (something you personally overcame), Profession (what you do or did for a day job), or Passion (what you're inherently interested in and can't get enough of). Pain-based businesses create missionaries who care deeply about the customer; market-based businesses create mercenaries who are in it for the money.

- Pain: something you went through and had to overcome personally
- Profession: any professional skill you do in a business can become a consulting or teaching business
- Passion: the articles you read, videos you watch, podcasts you listen to in spare time
- You don't need to pick perfectly on the first try -- picking anything begins the iteration process
- Goal is to get to your 'best bad idea' as fast as possible and make it less wrong over time

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Example: registered dietitian with 5,800 Instagram followers takes home almost $1M/year
- More than 1 out of 2 people who start a paid community on Skool make their first dollar

> "Many many businesses are started this way and in my opinion this tends to make mercenaries, this tends to make missionaries."
> "If you keep making a bad idea less wrong, eventually it becomes a good idea."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Profession: any professional skill you do in a business can become a consulting or teaching business
- You don't need to pick perfectly on the first try -- picking anything begins the iteration process
- Goal is to get to your 'best bad idea' as fast as possible and make it less wrong over time

## The Virtuous Cycle of Price (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Raising prices creates a self-reinforcing cycle: higher emotional investment leads to better results, less demanding clients, more profit to reinvest in value, and stronger sales conviction. Lowering prices creates the inverse vicious cycle.

- Higher price → higher emotional investment → higher perceived value → better results → less demanding clients
- More revenue per customer → more profit to reinvest in value → better product → higher justifiable price
- Sales team conviction increases when they believe price reflects value
- Lowering price creates opposite cycle: lower investment, lower results, higher demandingness, less profit, worse product
- Higher-paying clients are smarter, execute better, and require less hand-holding

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Portfolio company: raised price 50% → revenue grew from $450K/month to $1M/month, ROI from 5:1 to 10:1, profit nearly tripled in 8 weeks
- Close rate increased after 50% price increase — not decreased

> "Dan Kennedy: Charge as much as you can without cracking a smile."
> "Charge 10 times more for something 100 times as valuable."

## Three Ways to Make a Business More Valuable (Source: Alex Hormozi)

There are only three ways to increase business value: increase the number of customers, increase customer lifetime value (LTV/LGP), and decrease risk (increase reliability). Every allocation of time or money in your business must clearly map to one of these three or it's wasted.

- Get more customers
- Make customers worth more over time (LTV/LGP)
- Decrease risk to increase reliability of future earnings
- If you can't clearly state how an activity does one of these three, question whether it's worth doing
- Most people spend money on things that don't create value - that's why they don't get rich

> "Whenever you're allocating money or time in your business it has to circle up to: this is going to get me more customers, this is going to make them worth more, or this is somehow going to decrease risk"
> "If you can't very clearly state how you know it's going to get your customers, make them worth more, or decrease risk, then you have to ask yourself is this the best use of my time money and effort"

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Most people spend money on things that don't create value - that's why they don't get rich

## Three-Unit Business Audit Framework (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Diagnose a multi-unit business by isolating revenue, profit, and burn rate per unit to identify which units are profitable, break-even, or destroying capital. Units showing high burn with no path to profitability are candidates for immediate closure.

- Isolate P&L per business unit, not just blended totals
- Software unit had $40K revenue but -$110K profit ($7K/month burn) — destroying entire business
- Services unit had $131K revenue and $10K profit — the only profitable unit
- Education at $131K revenue was break-even without software cost allocation
- Blended view ($300K revenue, -$100K profit) hides which unit is the real problem

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Total revenue: $300,000; Total profit: -$100,000
- Software: $40K revenue, -$110K profit, $7K/month burn
- Services: $131K revenue, $10K profit
- Education: $131K revenue, break-even without software allocation

> "The software is losing $110,000 a year and burning $7,000 a month — shut it down immediately."
> "Business was not broken structurally; the monetization model was inefficient."

## Trim and Stack Offer Design (Source: Alex Hormozi)

List out all resources and assets at your disposal, then cut and combine them into the leanest, most valuable package. Don't offer a ton of stuff -- offer the few things people really want and remove everything else. Each bonus should independently be worth the price of the whole package.

- List all deliverables, then cut and combine for maximum value density
- Each bonus added should on its own be worth the price of the whole thing
- Simplicity makes for simpler marketing, simpler selling, and simpler delivery
- People decide to buy because of one specific detail that gets them over the hump -- make those details as compelling as possible
- Quote from Jack Dorsey: 'Make all the details perfect. Limit the number of details.'

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Business doing $480K/year with 43% profit margins, 42 couples, only 4 clients churned in 12 months
- Slack consumed ~40% of their time but was not driving renewals
- Recommended restructuring reduced delivery to ~10 hours per couple per year at $25K price

> "Each thing we add on its own should be worth the price of the whole thing."
> "Make all the details perfect. Limit the number of details."

## Two-Tiered Affiliate Prize Structure (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A tiered affiliate incentive system that rewards top performers with premium in-person experiences while providing long-tail affiliates with approximations of those benefits. Designed so both large-audience affiliates and micro-affiliates (3–10 referrals) participate profitably. Prizes must benefit the affiliate's audience, not just the affiliate.

- Top 10 affiliates receive fireside in-person one-on-one Q&A session where their audience submits questions in advance, incentivizing link clicks
- Top 50 affiliates receive three hours in-person at headquarters; top 10 also receive a dinner
- 10+ opt-in minimum threshold for activation; threshold scales based on friction level of referral method
- All 10+ affiliates receive: exclusive audiobook of 70+ profit tactics, raffle shot at 50 seats to day-long event, and event recording
- Non-monetary prizes can exceed simple commission percentages especially for low-priced products like books
- Using free prizes instead of cash avoids 1099 paperwork burden
- Every tier should offer an approximation of the premium experience, benefiting the affiliate's audience at each level

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 500,000 registrations for $100 Million Leads book launch
- Nearly one-third of registrants didn't know who Alex Hormozi was — referred by affiliates
- 33,000+ affiliates from prior year's launch
- Audiobook contains 70+ one-minute tactics on driving profit
- Top 50 event is a day-long session with 50 raffle seats

> "Affiliate prizes must benefit the affiliate's audience, not just the affiliate. If affiliates feel like they're hard-closing their audience, they protect their goodwill. Create a win-win-win for you, the affiliate, and their people."
> "Create tiers so killers with big audiences get premium benefits, while everyday people who can bring 3–10 friends also benefit. You want both the long tail and top performers."

- Don't lead customers to answers -- let them tell you in their own words
- Invest in what customers actually value, not what you think matters
- Most business owners spend enormous time solving problems that aren't problems

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**

- Glass wall at second location cost almost as much as entire first location

> "the vast majority of business owners spend a huge amount of time solving problems that aren't problems"

**Common Pitfalls:**

## Value-Based Pricing Reframe (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A mental model that decouples ethical concerns from pricing decisions. Price is determined by value delivered to the buyer and willingness to pay, not by the seller's financial state or self-perceived impostor status. Voluntary exchange at any price both parties agree on is ethically sound; undercharging while delivering real value is not virtuous.

- Impostor syndrome only applies when pretending to be something you're not — if you have the thing and they want the thing, there is no ethical issue
- Pricing should be based on value delivered to buyer, not on seller's financial situation
- Solution to perceived impostor syndrome: tell the exact truth about what you have and don't have, let buyer decide
- If at full capacity and not profitable, raise prices rather than adding locations/product lines
- Charging more forces development of sales skills that are required anyway to build a large business
- Both parties saying 'thank you' at the end of a transaction is the definition of correct pricing

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Case study: agency owner with 150 clients whose system worked but operations failed — solution was to sell the client-getting system with honest disclosure about operational limitations

> "If someone wants to pay you for something and you have the thing, and they're willing to exchange it — not holding a gun to them, it's voluntary exchange — that's how capitalism works."
> "You're not an impostor. You're broke. You only get impostor syndrome when you pretend to be something you're not."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Solution to perceived impostor syndrome: tell the exact truth about what you have and don't have, let buyer decide

## Value-Based Pricing vs. Cost-Plus Pricing (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Cost-plus pricing anchors price to COGS, systematically underpricing products because customer willingness-to-pay is independent of production cost. Value-based pricing instead sets price at what the market will bear, then tests downward only if conversion collapses.

- Cost-plus model means vendor cost increases directly suppress margins — broken dependency
- Customer willingness-to-pay has zero relationship to your COGS
- Strategy A: 5% monthly price increases across board — each increase roughly doubles profit at low margins
- Strategy B: A/B price test on highest-velocity SKUs, optimize for gross profit × units sold, not just conversion rate
- Raising price is risk-free if you can lower it again — run the test
- Previous owner sold $50 COGS product for $45; Cody nearly doubled prices on takeover

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Current net margin: 6.4%
- A 6% price increase roughly doubles profit at 6.4% net margins
- Compounding: 6% increase in month 1, then 6% on top in month 2 multiplies further
- Potential price increase range: 6-25% depending on segment

> "Often higher prices yield more total profit despite lower conversion. Optimize for gross profit × units sold, not just sales velocity."
> "Vendors' cost increases shouldn't affect retail price — customer willingness to pay is independent of COGS."

## Value-Price-Cost Spread (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Three lines that determine business health: value delivered (top), price charged (middle), and cost to deliver (bottom). You want maximum distance between all three. The gap between cost and price comes from doing the 100 small details your competitors won't do -- the unscalable micro-improvements that compound into premium positioning.

- Three lines: value (highest), price (middle), cost (lowest)
- Want maximum spread between all three
- The spread comes from the 100 tiny details competitors won't do
- Examples: greeting by name, waters in 30 seconds, bread in 2 minutes, asking about dinner pace
- Sometimes just asking the question (showing you care) improves the experience regardless of the answer
- No silver bullets -- it's 100 BBs of micro-improvements

> "you want these lines to be as far apart as humanly possible all of them you want them to be spread out super super far"

## What Would It Take to Be Number One (Source: Alex Hormozi)

A strategic thought exercise that bypasses incremental thinking by assuming the desired end state (market leadership) and reverse-engineering the required actions into the present. Asks 'What must be true for us to be #1?' then chains follow-up questions to identify the critical path.

- Replace 'How do we do 10% better?' with 'What would it take to be number one?'
- Follow with: 'What else would have to be true for us to be number one?'
- Assumes the end goal and reverse-engineers it into present actions
- Most businesses will identify one or two critical things that must happen
- Then ask: 'Do we have the resources? Why aren't we putting everything toward this?'
- Produces order-of-magnitude business growth rather than incremental gains
- Derived from Stephen Schwarzman's principle: building big vs. small is equally hard, payoffs differ enormously

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Chain of reasoning: #1 investment firm → need proprietary deal flow → need inbound deals → need massive brand → need content + books
- A successful restaurant caps at $1-2M annually; a top software company reaches billions—both are hard

> "Building a big business is hard. Building a small business is hard. Hard is a constant. You might as well go after something big."
> "Instead of asking 'How do we do 10% better?' ask: 'What would it take for us to be number one in our market?'"

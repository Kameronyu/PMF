## 70-20-10 Ad Creative Allocation (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Based on Google's innovation framework: allocate 70% of ad creative time to permutations of your proven winner, 20% to adjacent variations, and 10% to wild new ideas. In an 8-hour day: 5.5+ hours on winner permutations, 1.75 hours on adjacent ideas, 45 minutes on wild experiments.

- 70% of time: permutations of proven winning ad (black and white, sepia, reordered, different hooks/headlines/fonts/copy)
- 20% of time: adjacent variations similar in style but not the same
- 10% of time: completely wild new ideas
- Execute in reverse order: 10% wild ideas first, then 20% adjacent, then 70% permutations
- Use and abuse winning ads until they completely stop working

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Made about 100 versions of one winning ad (the 'kale ad')
- 8-hour day: ~5.5 hours on permutations, ~1.75 hours adjacent, ~45 minutes wild

> "When we had that ad I made about a hundred versions of that ad - black and white version, sepia filter, reordered the back end, swapped out hooks, different headlines, different subheads, changed fonts, different ad copy. I will use and abuse it until it doesn't work at all"

## 70/20/10 Rule (Steal From Yourself) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

70% of effort goes to carbon copying what works (stealing from yourself), 20% to adjacent variations one degree removed, 10% to completely new experiments. Most entrepreneurs flip this spending 70% on new ideas. Nike did not retire Just Do It after the second year.

- 70% carbon copy what already works
- 20% adjacent variations (same hook, different background/shirt/setting)
- 10% completely new experiments
- A building took two years to construct but five minutes to demolish
- Only one correct placement for each brick to create a building but unlimited to destroy

> "Nike didn't retire 'Just Do It' after the second year. When a hook works, you keep using it."

## 70/30 Brand Investment Ratio (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Allocate 70% of marketing spend to top-of-funnel brand awareness and 30% to bottom-of-funnel direct response. Based on the New Balance CMO case study, this ratio produces compounding returns after an initial 18-month dip, aligned with the 3:1 content-to-ads ratio studied across TV and radio.

- New Balance CMO flipped from 70% bottom-of-funnel / 30% top-of-funnel to 70% top / 30% bottom
- For the first 18 months, revenue went down after the flip
- On month 19, returns started climbing dramatically and continued compounding
- The 3:1 content-to-ads ratio is what keeps audiences engaged (every 4th post is an ad on Facebook)
- If you shift to 2:1, people stop watching and switch channels

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 70% top of funnel / 30% bottom of funnel optimal ratio
- 18 months before returns inflected upward
- 3:1 content-to-ads ratio is the studied standard across TV, radio, social media

> "I want to take our spend which right now is 70% bottom of funnel and 30% top of funnel and I want to flip these two things... after doing this for the first 18 months they made less money but on month 19 it started going up"
> "There's been a 3:1 ratio that's been studied by television, studied by radio - content to ads"

## Absolute vs. Relative Return (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Stop obsessing over ROAS (return on ad spend) and focus on absolute profit. Getting 10:1 ROAS on $1,500/month ($13,500 net) is worse than getting 4:1 ROAS on $15,000/month ($45,000 net). If you would do 2:1 on a million dollars and make a million in profit, that beats 10:1 on $10,000. Focus on how much total money you're making, not the ratio.

- Relative return (ROAS, ROI) declining while absolute profit grows is still a winning scenario
- Obsession with relative returns creates optimization loops that never produce 10x growth
- Conversion rate optimization (e.g., 30% → 45%) cannot produce 10x business growth
- 10x-ing inputs can produce 10x output even with lower relative efficiency
- High LTV:CAC ratios matter at early stage but become a ceiling trap at scale
- Relative return (multiple) goes down as you scale spend
- Absolute return (total profit dollars) goes up as you scale spend
- $10K in, $100K out = $90K profit vs $1M in, $2M out = $1M profit - choose the million
- *(+7 additional points)*

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Example: $100 → $1,000 (10:1) scaled to $200 → $1,200 (6:1) — absolute profit increased from $900 to $1,000
- Preferred: $1M spend → $2M return (2:1) over $10K spend → $100K return (10:1)
- $10K spend = $100K revenue ($90K profit) vs $1M spend = $2M revenue ($1M profit)
- Competitor spending $500/day (15K/month) at 4:1 = $45K net
- Hormozi spending $50-100/day (1.5K/month) at 10:1 = $13.5K net

> "If you give me $10,000 I can give you $100,000 - that's a great deal. If you give me a million dollars I can only make you an extra million. Which would you rather do? You want to give the million so you get two million"
> "When you're a small business owner, you get obsessed with relative returns... you will never 10x your business by getting this page from 30% to 300%. It's never going to happen. But you can 10x your inputs."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Obsession with relative returns creates optimization loops that never produce 10x growth
- High LTV:CAC ratios matter at early stage but become a ceiling trap at scale
- Don't optimize for ROAS percentage at the expense of total profit

## Ad Creative Volume Scaling Model (Source: Alex Hormozi)

You cannot scale ad spend with mediocre creative. A 5/10 ad can be profitable at $100/day but fails at $500/day because it only converts the hottest audience. To scale to $5,000-$50,000/day, you need 8-10/10 ads that convert cold audiences. This requires producing 30+ new unique video ads per week, permuted to ~100 pieces of creative.

- 5/10 ad = profitable at $100/day (hottest audience only)
- 7/10 ad = $500/day
- 8/10 ad = $5,000/day
- 9-10/10 ad = converts cold audiences worldwide
- Produce 30 new unique videos per week, permuted to ~100 creative pieces
- Typical struggling business: 5-7 ads per month vs 30+ per week
- If doing 100 ads/week vs competitor's 7/month = 50-60x volume advantage

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 30 new unique video ads per week
- 100 permuted creative pieces per week
- Competitors typically make 5-7 ads per month
- 50-60x volume advantage over typical competitor

> "Your ad is so bad it can only go to the absolute hottest audience and then as soon as it goes outside of that very hot audience it stops working"
> "In order to get that 10 out of 10 ad you have to do so much more preparation"

## Ad Fusion Reactor (Customer-Generated Content Machine) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Build the business so that normal operations automatically create marketing materials. Offer customers a $2,500 rebate for recording a video testimonial of their experience. This creates a self-sustaining cycle: your ads get a customer, customer has a great experience, experience gets documented, documented experience becomes the next ad.

- Offer rebate (e.g., $2,500) for testimonial videos -- add cost on top of price
- Customer videos should focus on experience, not money
- One initial marketing push starts the engine, then snowball rolls on its own
- Every business should have some mechanism for documenting stories, narratives, emotions, and outcomes

> "I'm a big believer in making ad machines in every business, which is like I want to have the normal function of the business create the marketing for the business. I want a fusion reactor."

## Ad Testing Budget: 2x Allowable CAC (Source: Alex Hormozi)

When testing new ads, budget 2x the cash collected from a customer in the first 30 days per campaign before shutting it off. This accounts for the inefficiency inherent in early testing while you find the hooks, creative, and copy variations that work. Once you find what converts, double down with 10 more variations of similar messaging.

- Budget 2x the 30-day cash collected per customer per campaign for testing
- You must stomach bad ads that don't convert to find good ads that do
- Once messaging converts, create 10 more variations of similar messaging and scale spend
- Don't spend too little -- underspending means insufficient volume to learn

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Business was spending only $10K/month on marketing against a $30K product

> "I budget two times the cash I collect from a customer in 30 days when I'm testing new ads."

**Common Pitfalls:**

## Advertising Inevitability (Two Forces Working Against You at Scale) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Two truths of scaling advertising that will never change: (1) the cost per eyeball (CPM) always goes up on every platform, and (2) as you scale, you reach colder audiences with lower conversion rates. The only way to combat these compounding headwinds is through referrals and product quality creating a compounding vehicle in your favor.

- CPMs always go up on every platform over time
- Scaling means reaching colder, less aware audiences with worse conversion rates
- These two forces compound against you linearly
- You need an equally strong compounding vehicle working FOR you: referrals
- If every customer brings 0.3 additional customers, you reduce acquisition cost by 30%
- Product quality drives referrals, which is the compounding force that offsets rising costs

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- If every customer brings 0.3 new customers, CAC decreases by 30%

> "you need an equally strong compounding vehicle that works in your favor to maintain your LTV to CAC ratio"
> "advertising will always become more expensive the cost per eyeball will always go up"

## Advertising vs. Branding Distinction (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Advertising and branding are distinct but interdependent functions: advertising is the process of making things known (distribution/reach), while branding is what you make known (the content of associations). You can have exceptional advertising with terrible branding, and vice versa.

- Advertising = the process of making known (the how/distribution)
- Branding = what you make known (the what/associations)
- Bud Light/Dylan Mulvaney example: exceptional advertising (massive reach) + bad branding (wrong association for core audience) = revenue decline
- Same association could be good branding for a different target audience (progressive brands)
- Every association trade-off gains some audience segments and loses others — goal is gaining more of desired who
- Four advertising methods: content creation, outreach to cold audience, outreach to warm audience, paid ads

> "Advertising is the process of making known. Branding is what you make known—it's the content of the advertising, it's what associations occur while you are making things known."
> "You can have exceptional advertising and terrible branding at the same time."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Bud Light/Dylan Mulvaney example: exceptional advertising (massive reach) + bad branding (wrong association for core audience) = revenue decline

## Agency Learning Method (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Use marketing agencies as paid education: tell them upfront you want to learn everything they do, offer to pay more and stay longer than average clients, then transition to a consulting period after absorbing their methods. Talk to at least 10 agencies before choosing one. The litmus test for readiness to decide: when you stop learning new things on calls.

- Tell agency: I want to steal everything you have, I'll pay more and stay longer
- Normal churn at month 3-4, offer to stay longer in exchange for transparency
- After absorbing methods, transition to consulting period for troubleshooting
- Talk to at least 10 agencies before buying - 15 calls to truly understand a space
- Litmus test: stop learning new things on calls = ready to make informed decision
- Red flag: agency doesn't provide best practices (funnel, scripts, response times)

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Takes ~15 calls with experts to wrap your head around a new space

> "I go to agencies and say: I want to steal everything that you have but I will do it over a longer period of time because it'll take me time to learn, and I will compensate you for the difference."

## Attention-to-Behavior Conversion Gap (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Grabbing attention and changing behavior are two different things. An ad can score 10/10 on attention (like the breast cancer screening ad) but still fail to actually change behavior if it leaves the viewer feeling good rather than feeling compelled to act. The point of persuasion is to change behavior, not just create positive sentiment.

- Attention and behavior change are separate objectives
- If an ad leaves you feeling good, it may not create urgency to act
- Showing what people will MISS OUT ON (loss aversion) is often more compelling
- Testimonials of someone who avoided disaster using the service drive more action
- Ad structure for behavior change: tons of attention + fact that makes uncomfortable + testimonial + CTA

> "The point of advertising is to let people know about your stuff, but the point of persuasion is to change behavior."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Testimonials of someone who avoided disaster using the service drive more action

## Avatar Alignment System (Ads, Lead Magnet, Price) (Source: Alex Hormozi)

To attract the right customer, you must align three things simultaneously: your ad messaging (speak their language), your lead magnet (match their identity), and your price point (match the economics of acquiring that avatar). Changing one without the others creates a broken funnel.

- Change ads to message the right prospect: 'tired of your job' attracts employees, 'frustrated with your business' attracts business owners
- Lead magnet must match desired avatar: a book about quitting your job attracts job-quitters, not business owners
- Price must match economics: cost of acquiring a business owner may be 5-10x more than a consumer, so price accordingly
- All three must change together or the economics won't work

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- 80% of the entrepreneur's revenue was from the wrong customer segment

> "If you can't say 'hey tired of your job' - that's going to attract people with jobs who are tired of their jobs. If you say 'frustrated with your business' then you're going to have different concerns"

The brand exists on a 4x4 matrix: direction (toward/away) and strength (high/low). One rotten flower (scandal, bad association) affects the entire bouquet. Brand moves are bets that you'll gain more desired audience than you lose.

- Separate your personal brand from your business brand
- Your personal brand stays with you for life
- Brand = bouquet of associations; remove the associations and there is no brand
- 4x4 matrix: direction (toward/away) x strength (high/low)

- One rotten flower (Dylan Mulvaney / Bud Light) affects entire bouquet

## CAC Optimization Front to Back (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Optimize CAC by working front to back in the funnel because earlier stages affect more people. A 1% click-through rate can become 3% (3x gain) but a 60% show rate cannot become 180%. The biggest gains come from improving advertising, offers, headlines, and team availability.

- Earlier funnel stages have order-of-magnitude improvement potential
- Later stages are constrained by 100% ceiling
- Exception: if one metric is a clear outlier far below industry average, fix that first
- Nailing the offer is the closest thing to a silver bullet for decreasing CAC
- Between different players in same industry, CAC is often similar - LTV is the differentiator

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Ad click-through rate can go from 1% to 3% (3x), but show rate cannot go from 60% to 180%

> "Your ability to conceptualize ideas is directly proportional to your vocabulary within a given domain."
> "The only thing that's the closest thing to a silver bullet in the CAC world is nailing your offer."

Social media has shifted from social media to interest media. The algorithm is so good it knows what you're talking about, what you're wearing, and your background, then displays it to people who watch similar content. - A grandma slapping you gets views but doesn't build influence with your target market
- If you sell services, you're an educator not an entertainer
- The content is the targeting - make content directly for your ideal customer

> "The content is the targeting. The algorithm is so good now. It knows what you're talking about. It judges your background, what you're wearing, who you are and will display it to the people they know have a history of watching content that is similar"

**Common Pitfalls:**

## Early Price Revelation Strategy (Source: Alex Hormozi)

Revealing high prices early in the sales funnel increases cost-per-call but improves back-end ROI by pre-qualifying prospects. Hiding price produces cheaper calls but worse revenue-per-call and lower overall return on advertising.

- Revealing price early filters out non-buyers before they consume sales resources
- People usually have an idea of a price range — the shock value prevents non-closers from booking
- Most operators measure front-end metrics (cost per call) instead of back-end metrics (revenue per call)
- Optimizing for cost per call is the wrong metric; optimize for return on advertising
- Early price revelation costs more per call but generates higher ROI overall

> "The more I reveal how expensive something is early in the process, the more money I make and the higher my return on advertising."

**Common Pitfalls:**
- Optimizing for cost per call is the wrong metric; optimize for return on advertising

- Cross-timezone audiences make timing less critical

- Email is effectively follow-up: you already paid to acquire the lead

- Email consumption is uniform across age groups 15-65+
- Not emailing is the single biggest mistake: one of the largest career errors was waiting years to start

**Key Metrics & Benchmarks:**
- Sent just under 10 million emails from acquisition.com in 90 days
- School.com got a 40% lift in conversion just from starting email follow-up
- 35x to 45x average ROI for email marketing
